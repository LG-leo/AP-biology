## 🧬 TOPIC 5.1 — Meiosis

### LO 5.1.A: Explain how meiosis results in the transmission of chromosomes from one generation to the next.

**Core Definition:**
Meiosis is a process that ensures the formation of **haploid gamete cells** (sometimes referred to as daughter cells) in **sexually reproducing diploid organisms**. Gametes contain half the number of chromosomes as somatic cells (n vs. 2n).

---

**MEIOSIS I — Detailed Steps**

**① Prophase I — The most complex and critical stage**

- **Homologous chromosomes pair up and condense**: The maternal and paternal homologous chromosomes approach each other and become shorter and thicker through coiling.
- **Synapsis occurs**: Homologous chromosomes align tightly to form a **bivalent** (also called a **tetrad** — 4 chromatids total).
- **Chiasmata may form**: At synapsis sites, **non-sister chromatids** may physically exchange segments.
  - ⚠️ Chiasmata are the structural basis of **crossing over (recombination)**, which generates genetic diversity (see Topic 5.2).
- **Meiotic spindle begins to form**.
- **Centrosomes move to opposite poles** of the cell.
- **Nuclear envelope breaks down**.

> 💡 **Key difference from mitosis**: In mitosis, homologous chromosomes do not pair up and synapsis does not occur.

**② Metaphase I**

- Spindle fibers (microtubules) attach to **kinetochores**.
- **Homologous pairs of chromosomes** (not individual chromosomes) align along the **metaphase plate** at the equator of the cell.
- ⚠️ **Critical distinction**: Each chromosome's kinetochore on one side attaches to spindle fibers from one pole, while its homologous chromosome's corresponding kinetochore attaches to fibers from the **opposite pole**.
- The orientation of each homologous pair is **random** — this is the basis of **independent assortment**.

**③ Anaphase I**

- **Homologous chromosomes separate** and are pulled toward opposite poles by spindle fibers.
- ⚠️ **Sister chromatids remain attached at the centromere** — this is the most critical difference from mitotic anaphase.
- The chromosome number is thereby **halved** (2n → 1n distributed to each pole).

**④ Telophase I**

- Spindle breaks down.
- Nuclear envelope re-forms (chromosomes may partially decondense at the poles).
- **Cytokinesis**:
  - **Animal cells**: **Cleavage furrow** forms.
  - **Plant cells**: **Cell plate** forms.
- **Result**: **2 haploid daughter cells** are formed (each with replicated chromosomes — each chromosome still has two sister chromatids).

---

**MEIOSIS II — Detailed Steps**

**① Prophase II**
- Spindle forms again.
- Sister chromatids attach to spindle fibers via the **centromere**.

**② Metaphase II**
- Chromosomes align along the metaphase plate.
- The **kinetochore** of each chromatid attaches to a microtubule extending from one pole.
- ⚠️ Nearly identical to mitotic metaphase.

**③ Anaphase II**
- **Proteins at the centromere break down**.
- **Sister chromatids are pulled apart** and move toward opposite poles.
- ⚠️ Only now does each chromatid become an **independent chromosome**.

**④ Telophase II**
- Spindle breaks down → nuclear envelope re-forms → chromatids decondense → cytokinesis occurs.
- **Result**: **4 haploid daughter cells**, each with **unduplicated chromatids (i.e., one chromosome each)**.

**Final product**: 4 genetically distinct haploid gametes (e.g., sperm or egg cells).

---

### LO 5.1.B: Describe similarities and differences between the phases and outcomes of mitosis and meiosis.

| Comparison Dimension | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---------------------|---------|---------|
| **Where it occurs** | Somatic cells (growth, repair, asexual reproduction) | Germ cells (gamete production) |
| **Number of divisions** | 1 | 2 (I + II) |
| **Number of daughter cells** | 2 | 4 |
| **Chromosome number in daughters** | 2n (diploid, same as parent) | 1n (haploid, half of parent) |
| **Genetic content of daughters** | Identical to parent (clones) | All different (recombination) |
| **Does pairing occur in prophase?** | No | **Yes (synapsis + crossing over)** |
| **Anaphase separation** | Sister chromatids separate | **Anaphase I: Homologs separate** (sister chromatids remain together) |
| **Function** | Growth, tissue repair, asexual reproduction | Gamete formation in sexual reproduction |
| **Similarity** | Both use a **spindle apparatus** to move chromosomes | Both use a **spindle apparatus** to move chromosomes |

## 🧬 TOPIC 5.2 — Meiosis and Genetic Diversity

### LO 5.2.A: Explain how the process of meiosis generates genetic diversity.

**THREE SOURCES OF GENETIC DIVERSITY:**

**① Crossing Over (Recombination)**
- **Timing**: **Prophase I** of meiosis
- **Location**: Between **non-sister chromatids** (i.e., one maternal and one paternal chromatid of a homologous pair)
- **Mechanism**: At chiasmata, non-sister chromatids exchange corresponding segments of DNA
- **Effect**: Creates **new combinations of alleles** → increases genetic diversity among resulting gametes

**② Independent Assortment (Random Assortment)**
- **Timing**: **Metaphase I** of meiosis
- **Mechanism**: The orientation of homologous pairs on the metaphase plate is **random**
  - The maternal chromosome from each pair can face either pole; the paternal chromosome faces the opposite pole
- **Calculation**: For n pairs of chromosomes, the number of possible combinations = **2ⁿ**
- **Humans (23 pairs)**: $2^{23} \approx 8.4 \times 10^6$ possible combinations
- ⚠️ The Law of Independent Assortment applies to genes located on **different chromosomes**

**③ Fertilization**
- Two haploid gametes fuse, restoring the diploid number
- Random selection of sperm (~200 million choose 1) + random selection of egg → combinatorial explosion
- **Possible zygote genotypes in humans**: ≈ $2^{23} \times 2^{23} \approx 7 \times 10^{13}$ possibilities

---

**NONDISJUNCTION — When Separation Goes Wrong**

- Occurs when **homologous chromosomes in Meiosis I** or **sister chromatids in Meiosis II** fail to separate properly
- **Consequences**:
  - Some gametes gain an extra chromosome (n+1)
  - Some gametes lack a chromosome (n-1)
- After fertilization → **aneuploidy** (abnormal chromosome number), such as trisomy or monosomy

> ❌ **Exclusion**: Details of sexual reproduction cycles in various plants and animals are beyond the scope of the AP Exam.

## 🧬 TOPIC 5.3 — Mendelian Genetics

### LO 5.3.A: Explain the inheritance of genes and traits as described by Mendel's laws.

**MENDEL'S FIRST LAW: LAW OF SEGREGATION**
- Each individual carries two alleles for each gene; these alleles **segregate (separate)** during gamete formation
- Each gamete receives **only one** of the two alleles
- **Basis**: **Separation of homologous chromosomes** in Anaphase I of meiosis

**MENDEL'S SECOND LAW: LAW OF INDEPENDENT ASSORTMENT**
- Genes located on **different chromosomes** assort independently during gamete formation
- **Basis**: **Random alignment of homologous pairs** in Metaphase I of meiosis
- ⚠️ Genes on the same chromosome (**linked genes**) do **not** follow independent assortment

---

**CORE TERMINOLOGY — Precise Definitions**

| Term | Definition | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Genotype** | The set of alleles an individual inherits for one or more genes | Can be homozygous or heterozygous |
| **Phenotype** | The observable expression of inherited traits | The result of genotype + environment interaction |
| **Homozygous** | Both alleles are the same (e.g., AA or aa) | |
| **Heterozygous** | The two alleles are different (e.g., Aa) | |
| **Dominant** | An allele that is expressed even when heterozygous | Usually denoted by uppercase letter |
| **Recessive** | An allele that is only expressed when homozygous | Usually denoted by lowercase letter |

---

**HYBRIDIZATION ANALYSIS TOOLS**

1. **Monohybrid Cross**: Tracks one trait → F₂ phenotypic ratio **3:1**
2. **Dihybrid Cross**: Tracks two traits → F₂ phenotypic ratio **9:3:3:1** (assuming independent assortment)
3. **Test Cross**: Crossing an individual of unknown genotype with a **homozygous recessive** → offspring phenotypes reveal the parent's genotype
4. **Punnett Squares**: Used to predict genotypes and phenotypes of parents and offspring
5. **Pedigree Analysis**: Family tree analysis to infer inheritance patterns (autosomal dominant/recessive, X-linked dominant/recessive, Y-linked)

**PROBABILITY RULES (Explicitly Required in the Course Framework)**

| Situation | Rule | Formula |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Mutually exclusive events** | Probability of event A **or** event B occurring | $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)$ |
| **Independent events** | Probability of event A **and** event B both occurring | $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$ |

## 🧬 TOPIC 5.4 — Non-Mendelian Genetics

### LO 5.4.A: Explain deviations from Mendel's model of the inheritance of traits.

**SIX TYPES OF DEVIATIONS — Detailed Explanations**

---

**① Genetic Linkage**

- **Definition**: Genes located on the **same chromosome** tend to be inherited together during meiosis
- **Consequence**: Do not follow the Law of Independent Assortment → offspring show **far more parental types** than recombinant types
- **Genetic Mapping**:
  - **Recombination frequency** is used to calculate the **relative distance** between genes on a chromosome
  - **1% recombination frequency = 1 map unit = 1 centiMorgan (cM)**
  - Formula: $\text{Recombination frequency} = \frac{\text{Number of recombinant offspring}}{\text{Total offspring}} \times 100\%$
  - Lower recombination frequency between two genes → closer together → tighter linkage

---

**② Codominance**

- **Definition**: Both alleles are **fully and simultaneously expressed** in the heterozygote
- The heterozygote's phenotype is **not** intermediate — it is the **sum** of both homozygous phenotypes
- **Classic examples**:
  - **ABO blood type AB** ($I^AI^B$) → both A and B antigens are present on red blood cells
  - Roan coat color in cattle (red and white hairs intermingled)

---

**③ Incomplete Dominance**

- **Definition**: Neither allele can completely mask the other
- The heterozygote's phenotype is an **intermediate ("blended") version** of the two homozygous phenotypes
- **Classic examples**:
  - **Snapdragon flowers**: Red (RR) × White (rr) → Pink (Rr)
  - Human hair texture: Curly (CC) × Straight (cc) → Wavy (Cc)

> ⚠️ **Codominance vs Incomplete Dominance**: Codominance = both appear simultaneously (AB blood); Incomplete dominance = blending (pink flower).

---

**④ Sex-Linked Inheritance**

- Traits determined by genes on **sex chromosomes** (X or Y)
- **Key features of X-linked recessive inheritance**:
  - **XY individuals (males)** have only one X chromosome → receiving a single recessive allele causes the trait
  - **XX individuals (females)** need two recessive alleles to express the trait
  - Therefore: **Incidence in XY individuals is much higher than in XX individuals**
  - A father cannot pass X-linked traits to his sons (sons inherit Y from father)
- **Classic examples**: Red-green color blindness, Hemophilia A, Duchenne muscular dystrophy
- **Non-X/Y sex determination systems** (mentioned in the framework):
  - **Birds**: **ZW system** (ZZ = male, ZW = female)
  - **Bees**: **Haplodiploidy** (diploid = female, haploid = male)

---

**⑤ Pleiotropy**

- **Definition**: The expression of a **single gene** results in **multiple seemingly unrelated phenotypic traits**
- These traits therefore do **not segregate independently**
- **Classic examples**:
  - **Sickle Cell Disease**:
    - A single mutation in the HBB gene → abnormal hemoglobin structure
    - Results in: sickle-shaped red blood cells, anemia, pain crises, spleen damage, **and** partial resistance to malaria
  - **Marfan Syndrome**:
    - FBN1 gene mutation → abnormal connective tissue
    - Results in: unusually tall height, long limbs, lens dislocation, aortic aneurysm risk
  - **Phenylketonuria (PKU)**: PAH gene mutation → multiple neurological developmental abnormalities

---

**⑥ Non-nuclear Inheritance (Cytoplasmic Inheritance)**

- Mitochondria and chloroplasts contain their own DNA, encoding some proteins
- **Key features**:
  - These organelles are **randomly assorted** to gametes and daughter cells
  - Do **not** follow Mendelian segregation and independent assortment patterns
  - Exhibit **maternal inheritance**

| Organism Type | Transmission Mode | Mitochondria from | Chloroplasts from |
|--------------|-------------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Animals** | Mitochondria are transmitted **by the egg**, **not by sperm** | **Mother** | N/A |
| **Plants** | Mitochondria and chloroplasts are transmitted **in the ovule**, **not in the pollen** | **Mother** | **Mother** |

- **Classic examples**:
  - Human mitochondrial diseases (Leber's Hereditary Optic Neuropathy, MELAS syndrome)
  - Leaf color inheritance in **Mirabilis jalapa** (four o'clock plant)

## 🧬 TOPIC 5.5 — Environmental Effects on Phenotype

### LO 5.5.A: Explain how the same genotype can result in multiple phenotypes under different environmental conditions.

**PHENOTYPIC PLASTICITY**

- **Definition**: The ability of a single genotype to produce **different phenotypes** under different environmental conditions
- **Mechanism**: Environmental conditions **influence gene expression** (through regulation of transcription, translation, protein modification, etc.)
- Phenotype = **Genotype + Environment**

**💡 Illustrative Examples from the Course Framework**:

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Height and weight in humans** | Significantly influenced by environmental factors (nutrition, exercise, etc.) |
| **Flower color based on soil pH** | e.g., Hydrangea: acidic soil → blue flowers; alkaline soil → pink flowers |
| **Seasonal fur color in arctic animals** | e.g., Arctic fox: white in winter (snow camouflage), brown/gray in summer |
| **Sex determination in reptiles** | e.g., Sea turtles, crocodiles: **Temperature-dependent sex determination (TSD)** — incubation temperature determines offspring sex |
| **Effect of increased UV on melanin production** | UV exposure increases melanin production in animals (including humans — tanning) |
| **Presence of opposite mating type on pheromone production** | In yeast and other fungi, the presence of the opposite mating type influences pheromone production |

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.1 — DNA and RNA Structure

### LO 6.1.A: Describe the structures involved in passing hereditary information from one generation to the next.

**CHEMICAL FORM OF GENETIC MATERIAL**
- Genetic information is stored in **DNA molecules**; in some viruses, **RNA** serves as the genetic material

**CHROMOSOME STRUCTURE COMPARISON**

| Feature | Prokaryotes | Eukaryotes |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **Chromosome shape** | **Circular**, typically one chromosome | **Multiple linear chromosomes** |
| **Location** | Nucleoid region (no nuclear membrane) | Inside the nucleus (enclosed by nuclear membrane) |
| **DNA packaging** | No histones (though histone-like proteins exist) | DNA bound to **histones** and associated proteins → **chromatin** |
| **Extra DNA** | May contain **plasmids** — small circular extra-chromosomal DNA | Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA |

---

### LO 6.1.B: Describe the characteristics of DNA that allow it to be used as hereditary material.

**NUCLEOTIDE STRUCTURE**
Each nucleotide consists of three parts:
1. **Phosphate group**
2. **Five-carbon sugar**: DNA = deoxyribose; RNA = ribose
3. **Nitrogenous base**

**PURINES vs PYRIMIDINES**

| Category | Bases | Ring Structure | Pairing Rule |
|----------|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Purines** | Adenine (**A**), Guanine (**G**) | **Double ring** | Purine pairs with pyrimidine |
| **Pyrimidines** | Cytosine (**C**), Thymine (**T** — DNA only), Uracil (**U** — RNA only) | **Single ring** | Pyrimidine pairs with purine |

**BASE PAIRING RULES (Chargaff's Rules)**

| Molecule | Pairing | Number of Hydrogen Bonds |
|----------|---------|-------------------------|
| **DNA** | A = T; G ≡ C | A-T: 2 H-bonds; G-C: 3 H-bonds |
| **RNA** | A = U; G ≡ C | A-U: 2 H-bonds; G-C: 3 H-bonds |

- Base pairing is **highly conserved** through evolution
- DNA strands are **antiparallel**: one strand runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.2 — DNA Replication

### LO 6.2.A: Describe the mechanisms by which genetic information is copied for transmission between generations.

**BASIC PRINCIPLES OF DNA REPLICATION**

1. **Directionality**: DNA polymerase can only synthesize new DNA in the **5' → 3'** direction
2. **Semiconservative Replication**:
   - The parental DNA double helix unwinds; each strand serves as a **template** for a new complementary strand
   - Each daughter DNA molecule contains **one parental strand** and **one newly synthesized strand**
   - This was proven by the **Meselson-Stahl experiment (1958)**

**DETAILED STEPS OF DNA REPLICATION**

| Step | Player | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| **① Unwinding** | **Helicase** | Unwinds the DNA double helix, creating the **replication fork** |
| **② Relaxing supercoiling** | **Topoisomerase** | **Relaxes supercoiling** ahead of the replication fork, preventing DNA tangling |
| **③ Providing a starting point** | **Primase (a type of RNA polymerase)** | Synthesizes **RNA primers** to provide a 3'-OH end for DNA polymerase to start from |
| **④ Synthesizing new strand** | **DNA Polymerase** | Reads the template strand and adds complementary nucleotides |
| **⑤ Joining fragments** | **Ligase** | Joins **Okazaki fragments** on the **lagging strand** |

**LEADING STRAND vs LAGGING STRAND**

| Feature | Leading Strand | Lagging Strand |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Direction of synthesis** | Continuous, toward the replication fork | **Discontinuous**, away from the replication fork |
| **Number of primers needed** | 1 (at the start) | Multiple (one per Okazaki fragment) |
| **Product** | Continuous long DNA strand | Series of **Okazaki fragments** |
| **Joining** | Not required | Requires **ligase** to join fragments |

> ❌ **Exclusion**: The names of specific steps and enzymes beyond DNA polymerase, ligase, RNA polymerase, helicase, and topoisomerase are beyond the scope of the AP Exam.

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.3 — Transcription and RNA Processing

### LO 6.3.A: Describe the mechanisms by which genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to protein.

**THREE TYPES OF RNA AND THEIR FUNCTIONS**

| RNA Type | Full Name | Function | Location |
|----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **mRNA** | Messenger RNA | Carries genetic information from DNA in the nucleus to ribosomes in the cytoplasm | Synthesized in nucleus → exported to cytoplasm |
| **tRNA** | Transfer RNA | Carries specific amino acids to the ribosome; has an **anticodon** that pairs with mRNA codons | Cytoplasm |
| **rRNA** | Ribosomal RNA | Functional building blocks of ribosomes (catalyzes peptide bond formation) | Ribosomes |

---

**TRANSCRIPTION PROCESS — Detailed Steps**

1. **Initiation**: RNA polymerase binds to the **promoter region** of DNA, locally unwinding the double helix
2. **Elongation**:
   - RNA polymerase reads the **DNA template strand in the 3'→5' direction**
   - Synthesizes the RNA molecule in the **5'→3' direction**
   - Base pairing rules: A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C
3. **Termination**: RNA polymerase reaches a **termination sequence** and releases the newly synthesized RNA molecule

**⚠️ Key Distinction**:
- **Template strand** (antisense strand): The strand read by RNA polymerase
- **Coding strand** (sense strand): Has the same sequence as the RNA (T→U)

---

**EUKARYOTIC RNA PROCESSING (3 Major Modifications)**

**① 5' Capping**
- A modified **guanine nucleotide (GTP cap)** is added to the 5' end of the mRNA
- Functions: Helps ribosome recognize mRNA; protects from exonuclease degradation

**② Poly-A Tail Addition**
- **50-250 adenine nucleotides** are added to the 3' end
- Functions: Increases mRNA stability; promotes nuclear export; helps translation initiation

**③ RNA Splicing**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Introns** | **Non-coding** sequences that are **removed** |
| **Exons** | **Coding** sequences that are **retained** and joined together |

- **Alternative Splicing**:
  - The same pre-mRNA can be spliced in different ways, producing **multiple different mature mRNA versions**
  - **One gene → multiple protein isoforms**
  - Greatly increases proteome diversity without increasing gene number
  - ⚠️ This is a **key concept specifically emphasized in the framework**!

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.4 — Translation

### LO 6.4.A: Explain how the phenotype of an organism is determined by its genotype.

**SITE OF TRANSLATION**
- Translation occurs on **ribosomes**
- **Prokaryotes**: Ribosomes in the cytoplasm
- **Eukaryotes**: Ribosomes in the cytoplasm **or** on the surface of the **rough endoplasmic reticulum (rough ER)**

**PROKARYOTIC SPECIAL FEATURE — Coupled Transcription-Translation**
- In prokaryotes, **translation begins before transcription is complete**
- Ribosomes can bind to the 5' end of mRNA while the mRNA is still being synthesized from DNA
- This is possible because prokaryotes lack a nuclear membrane separating transcription from translation

---

**THREE STEPS OF TRANSLATION — Detailed**

**① Initiation**
1. Small ribosomal subunit binds to mRNA
2. The ribosome recognizes the **start codon AUG** (coding for **methionine**)
3. Initiator tRNA (methionine-tRNA) binds to the start codon
4. Large ribosomal subunit joins, forming the complete ribosome

**② Elongation**
1. **Codon recognition**: The ribosome reads mRNA in **triplet codons**
2. **tRNA delivery**: A tRNA with the matching anticodon brings the correct amino acid to the A site
3. **Peptide bond formation**: The ribosome (rRNA acts as a **ribozyme** — peptidyl transferase) catalyzes peptide bond formation between amino acids
4. **Translocation**: The ribosome moves 3 nucleotides (one codon) along the mRNA
5. The process repeats

**③ Termination**
1. The ribosome encounters a **stop codon** (UAA, UAG, or UGA)
2. No tRNA recognizes stop codons; a **release factor** binds instead
3. The polypeptide chain is released from the tRNA
4. Ribosome dissociates; the newly synthesized protein is released

---

**KEY FEATURES OF THE GENETIC CODE**

- **Degeneracy (Redundancy)**: Most amino acids are encoded by **more than one codon** (e.g., Leucine has 6 codons)
- **Universality**: Nearly all living organisms use **the same genetic code** → This is powerful evidence for **common ancestry**! ⭐

> ❌ **Exclusion**: Memorization of the genetic code chart (except the start codon AUG) is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.

**RETROVIRUSES — A Special Case of Information Flow**
- Normal flow: DNA → RNA → Protein
- Retrovirus flow: **RNA → DNA → RNA → Protein**
- Enzyme: **Reverse Transcriptase**
  - Copies the viral RNA genome into DNA
  - This DNA **integrates into the host genome**
  - Uses host cell's transcription/translation machinery to produce new viral progeny
- **Classic example**: HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.5 — Regulation of Gene Expression

### LO 6.5.A: Describe the types of interactions that regulate gene expression.

**MULTIPLE LEVELS OF REGULATION**

| Level | Prokaryotes | Eukaryotes |
|-------|------------|------------|
| **Transcriptional** | Operon system (primary) | Transcription factors, enhancers, silencers, chromatin remodeling |
| **Post-transcriptional** | Less common | RNA splicing, RNA editing, RNA stability |
| **Translational** | Less common | Translation regulatory factors, mRNA localization |
| **Post-translational** | Less common | Protein modification, degradation |

---

**KEY CONCEPTS**

1. **Regulatory Sequences**: Stretches of DNA that interact with regulatory proteins to control whether transcription occurs
2. **Constitutive Expression**: Some genes are expressed in all cells at all times (e.g., "housekeeping genes")
3. **Inducible Expression**: Some genes are turned on only when needed

---

**EPIGENETIC REGULATION**

- **Definition**: **Reversible modifications** to DNA or histones that affect gene expression **without changing the DNA sequence**
- **Two main mechanisms**:

| Mechanism | Description | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **DNA Methylation** | Addition of methyl (-CH₃) groups to cytosine (C) bases | Usually **represses** gene expression (silences genes) |
| **Histone Modification** | Acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation of histone tails | Acetylation → **activates**; Deacetylation → **represses** |
| **Chromatin Remodeling** | Changes in how tightly DNA is wound around histones | Loosely packed → accessible for transcription; Tightly packed → inaccessible |

---

**GENE EXPRESSION AND PHENOTYPE — Core Framework Concepts**

1. **Cell Differentiation**: All somatic cells contain the same DNA, but **differential gene expression** leads to cell specialization
2. **Tissue-specific Proteins**: Different cell types express different combinations of genes → produce different proteins → perform different functions
3. **Sequential Expression During Development**: Induction of transcription factors → triggers cascading gene expression
4. **Function and Amount of Gene Products** determine the phenotype of organisms

---

### LO 6.5.B: Explain how the location of regulatory sequences relates to their function.

**PROKARYOTES: THE OPERON SYSTEM**

- **Operon** = A group of genes coordinately regulated at the transcriptional level
- **Structure**:
  - **Promoter**: RNA polymerase binding site
  - **Operator**: Regulatory protein binding site
  - **Structural genes**: The coding genes that are co-expressed
- **Inducible System**: e.g., **lac operon**
  - Normally off; turned on when lactose is present
- **Repressible System**: e.g., **trp operon**
  - Normally on; turned off when tryptophan is present

> ⚠️ The framework specifically notes that students can use the **lac operon** to understand **positive and negative gene regulation**.

**EUKARYOTES: COORDINATE REGULATION**
- Groups of genes may be influenced by the **same transcription factors**
- These genes are not necessarily adjacent on the chromosome
- Example: Multiple genes responding to the same hormone signal

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.6 — Gene Expression and Cell Specialization

### LO 6.6.A: Explain how the binding of transcription factors to promoter regions affects gene expression and the phenotype of the organism.

**MOLECULAR MECHANISMS OF TRANSCRIPTION INITIATION**

1. **Positive Regulation**:
   - **Transcription factors** bind to **promoter** or **enhancer** sequences
   - Enhancers can be located **upstream or downstream** of the transcription start site, even thousands of base pairs away
   - Through **DNA looping**, enhancers contact the promoter region → activates transcription
   - Recruits RNA polymerase → initiates transcription

2. **Negative Regulation**:
   - **Repressor proteins** bind to **silencer** sequences or the operator
   - Prevents RNA polymerase from binding or elongating → **inhibits transcription**
   - Example: lac repressor in the lac operon

---

### LO 6.6.B: Explain the connection between the regulation of gene expression and phenotypic differences in cells and organisms.

- **Differential gene expression** → different cells in the same organism produce different proteins → achieve different functions
- Example: Nerve cells express neurotransmitter-related genes; muscle cells express actin/myosin genes
- **Role of small RNA molecules** (explicitly required by the framework):
  - **miRNA (microRNA)**: ~22 nt small RNA; **partially complementary** to target mRNA → inhibits translation or promotes mRNA degradation
  - **siRNA (small interfering RNA)**: ~20-25 nt; **fully complementary** to target mRNA → leads to mRNA degradation (RNA interference)

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.7 — Mutations

### LO 6.7.A: Describe the various types of mutation.

**MUTATION TYPES CLASSIFIED BY MOLECULAR CHANGE**

| Mutation Type | Definition | Changes Reading Frame? | Effect on Protein |
|--------------|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| **Point Mutation** | One nucleotide is substituted for another | **No** | Depends on the specific substitution |
| —— **Silent Mutation** | Nucleotide change but same amino acid | No | **No change** |
| —— **Missense Mutation** | Nucleotide change → different amino acid | No | May alter protein structure/function |
| —— **Nonsense Mutation** | Becomes a stop codon → **premature termination** | No (but truncates protein) | **Usually loss of function** |
| **Frameshift Mutation** | Insertion or deletion of one or more nucleotides | **Changes reading frame** ⭐ | Usually produces a completely different, non-functional protein |

> ⚠️ **Common Student Misconceptions (explicitly noted in the framework)**:
> 1. Students often think all mutations are harmful — **WRONG!** Mutations can be beneficial, detrimental, **or neutral**
> 2. Students often say "mutations cause protein denaturation" — **WRONG!** Denaturation refers to conformational change, not mutation
> 3. **Silent mutations** prove that not all point mutations change the protein

---

**THREE POSSIBLE OUTCOMES OF MUTATIONS**

| Outcome | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Beneficial** | Increases fitness in a particular environment | MC1R gene mutation → adaptive melanism in pocket mice |
| **Detrimental** | Decreases fitness | CFTR gene mutation → cystic fibrosis |
| **Neutral** | No significant effect on fitness | Silent mutation (same amino acid) |

> ❌ **Exclusion**: Knowledge of specific mutations and their effects is beyond the scope (the above are illustrative examples only).

---

### LO 6.7.B: Explain how changes in genotype may result in changes in phenotype.

**SOURCES OF MUTATIONS (3 Categories)**

1. **DNA replication errors**: DNA polymerase occasionally incorporates the wrong nucleotide
   - Cells have **mismatch repair** mechanisms, but they cannot fix 100% of errors
2. **Defects in DNA repair mechanisms**: Mutations in repair system genes → reduced repair capacity
3. **External factors (Mutagens)**:
   - **Physical agents**: Ionizing radiation (X-rays, γ-rays), UV radiation (forms pyrimidine dimers)
   - **Chemical agents**: Reactive chemicals, nitrites, aflatoxins, etc.

---

**CHROMOSOMAL NUMBER ABNORMALITIES — Aneuploidy**

| Type | Definition | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Trisomy** | Three copies of one chromosome (2n+1) | **Nondisjunction** during meiosis |
| **Monosomy** | Only one copy of one chromosome (2n-1) | **Nondisjunction** during meiosis |

- Aneuploidy usually results in **developmental limitations** or lethality
- In humans, most aneuploidies are lethal (early miscarriage)
- A few are survivable: Down syndrome (Trisomy 21), Turner syndrome (XO), Klinefelter syndrome (XXY)
- Nondisjunction can occur in **Meiosis I** (homologs fail to separate) or **Meiosis II** (sister chromatids fail to separate)

> ❌ **Exclusion**: Knowledge of specific disorders related to changes in chromosome number is beyond the scope.

### LO 6.7.C: Explain how alterations in DNA sequences contribute to variation that can be subject to natural selection.

**MUTATIONS ARE THE RAW MATERIAL FOR EVOLUTION**
- Mutations are the **ultimate source of all genetic variation**
- No mutations → no new alleles → natural selection has nothing to act upon → evolution stops

---

**FOUR MECHANISMS OF GENETIC VARIATION IN PROKARYOTES (Horizontal Gene Transfer)**

| Mechanism | Description | Direction |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Transformation** | Cells take up **free DNA** from the environment (e.g., from dead bacteria) | **Horizontal gene transfer** |
| **Transduction** | **Viruses (bacteriophages)** carry DNA from one bacterium to another | **Horizontal gene transfer** |
| **Conjugation** | Two bacteria connect via a **sex pilus** and transfer plasmid or DNA fragments directly | **Horizontal gene transfer** |
| **Transposition** | DNA segments ("jumping genes" / transposons) **move positions** within or between DNA molecules | Can cause gene rearrangement |

- **Viral recombination**: Related viruses infecting the same host cell can **recombine genetic information**
- These mechanisms that increase genetic variation are **evolutionarily conserved** and shared by various organisms

## 🧬 TOPIC 6.8 — Biotechnology

### LO 6.8.A: Explain the use of genetic engineering techniques in analyzing or manipulating DNA.

**FOUR CORE TECHNOLOGIES**

---

**① Gel Electrophoresis**

| Parameter | Principle |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Separation basis** | DNA fragment **size** and **charge** |
| **Basic procedure** | Load DNA samples into wells in an agarose gel → apply electric current → DNA (negatively charged) moves toward positive electrode → smaller fragments move faster and farther |
| **Result** | **DNA band pattern (DNA fingerprint)** |
| **Applications** | Forensic identification, paternity testing, species identification, phylogenetic analysis |

---

**② Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**

- Purpose: **Exponential amplification** of a specific DNA segment
- **Cycle of 3 steps (repeated 25-35 times)** :

| Step | Temperature | Process |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Denaturation** | ~95°C | Double-stranded DNA unwinds into single strands |
| **Annealing** | ~50-65°C | Primers bind specifically to target DNA sequences |
| **Extension** | ~72°C | DNA polymerase (Taq polymerase) synthesizes new strand from the primer |

- Each cycle **doubles** the amount of target DNA

---

**③ Bacterial Transformation**

- Introducing **foreign DNA** (e.g., recombinant plasmid) into bacterial cells
- Commonly used bacterium: **E. coli**
- **Selectable marker**: Usually includes an **antibiotic resistance gene** (e.g., amp^R) to identify successfully transformed bacteria
- **Applications**:
  - **Gene cloning**: Insert foreign DNA fragment into plasmid → transform bacteria → mass produce
  - **Recombinant protein production**: e.g., human insulin, growth hormones
  - **Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs)**: Introducing foreign genes into plant/animal genomes

---

**④ DNA Sequencing**

- Determines the **exact order of nucleotides** in a DNA molecule
- Modern high-throughput sequencing can rapidly read massive amounts of sequence data
- **Applications**:
  - Comparing DNA sequences across species → **phylogenetic analysis**
  - Forensic identification
  - Genetic disease diagnosis
  - Genomics research

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.1 — Introduction to Natural Selection

### LO 7.1.A: Describe the causes of natural selection.

**DARWIN'S CORE LOGICAL CHAIN**

```
Overproduction
      ↓
Limited Resources → Struggle for Existence
      ↓
Variation among Individuals
      ↓
Favorable Variations → Higher Survival → More Offspring
(Differential Survival & Reproduction)
      ↓
Favorable Traits Passed to Subsequent Generations
(Descent with Modification)
      ↓
Change in Population's Genetic Makeup Over Time → Evolution
```

**KEY STATEMENTS (Exam Attention)**

- **Natural selection is a major mechanism of evolution** — but not the only one (genetic drift, gene flow, etc.)
- **Competition**: Due to limited resources (food, mates, space, etc.), individuals inevitably compete
- **Differential Survival**: Individuals with more favorable phenotypes are more likely to survive
- **Differential Reproduction**: Surviving individuals produce more offspring, passing favorable traits to subsequent generations

---

### LO 7.1.B: Explain how natural selection affects populations.

**EVOLUTIONARY FITNESS**
- ⚠️ **Fitness ≠ physical strength or health**
- **Evolutionary fitness = reproductive success**
  - Measured by: An individual's **relative contribution** to the next generation's gene pool
  - Even a very strong individual has **zero fitness** if it produces no offspring

**EFFECT OF ENVIRONMENTAL FLUCTUATION**
- **Biotic environment**: Predators, competitors, parasites, food sources, etc.
- **Abiotic environment**: Temperature, precipitation, sunlight, soil composition, etc.
- These environmental factors **fluctuate** → affecting the **rate and direction** of evolution
- **Different genetic variations may be selected in each generation** — what is advantageous today may be disadvantageous tomorrow

> ⚠️ **Framework's Explicit Warnings**:
> 1. Students must avoid **Lamarckian descriptions** (e.g., "organisms evolved a trait because they needed it")
> 2. Use terms like "fitness" carefully — must explain the underlying concept of **reproductive success**
> 3. Be clear: **Natural selection acts on individuals, but populations evolve**

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.2 — Natural Selection

### LO 7.2.A: Describe the importance of phenotypic variation in a population.

**NATURAL SELECTION ACTS ON PHENOTYPIC VARIATION**

- **Prerequisite**: The population must contain **heritable phenotypic variation**
- Natural selection **cannot create new variation** — it can only **filter existing variation**
- Environmental changes create **selective pressure** → certain phenotypes are favored

**TYPES OF SELECTION (Framework Requires Understanding)**

| Selection Type | Description | Effect on Population |
|---------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Directional Selection** | Favors one extreme phenotype | Population mean shifts in one direction |
| **Stabilizing Selection** | Favors intermediate phenotypes, eliminates extremes | Genetic diversity reduced; population remains stable |
| **Disruptive Selection** | Favors both extreme phenotypes, eliminates intermediates | May lead to **speciation** |

---

### LO 7.2.B: Explain how variation in molecules within cells connects to the fitness of an organism.

- Different individuals of the same species show variation in the **number and types of molecules** within their cells
- These molecular-level variations determine an organism's ability to survive and reproduce in a particular environment
- Populations with **greater molecular diversity** → greater ability to respond to environmental changes

**💡 Illustrative Examples from the Framework**:
- **Flowering time in relation to global climate change**: Some plants flower earlier due to warming → if this becomes mismatched with pollinator activity → reduced reproductive success
- **DDT resistance in insects**: Before DDT application, a few insects carry resistance genes → DDT kills non-resistant individuals → resistant individuals reproduce → population develops resistance
- **Sickle cell anemia**:
  - Homozygous (Hb^S Hb^S): severe anemia, low fitness
  - Homozygous (Hb^A Hb^A): normal, but susceptible to malaria in malaria-endemic regions
  - **Heterozygous (Hb^A Hb^S)**: mild anemia but **partial resistance to malaria** → **highest fitness in malarial regions** ← Heterozygote advantage

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.3 — Artificial Selection

### LO 7.3.A: Explain how humans can affect diversity within a population.

**ARTIFICIAL SELECTION = HUMAN-DIRECTED EVOLUTION**

- Humans **intentionally select individuals with desirable traits** for breeding
- Over successive generations → significant changes in traits accumulate
- **Difference from natural selection**: The selective pressure comes from **human preferences** rather than natural conditions

**CLASSIC EXAMPLES**

| Domain | Example | Result of Selection |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| **Agriculture** | Maize from teosinte (wild grass) | Larger kernels, regular rows, higher yield |
| **Animal husbandry** | Dog domestication | From wolves to hundreds of breeds (different sizes, coat colors, temperaments) |

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.4 — Population Genetics

### LO 7.4.A: Explain how random occurrences affect the genetic makeup of a population.

**RANDOM PROCESSES THAT DRIVE EVOLUTION**

---

**① Mutation**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Randomness** | Mutations are not guided by an organism's "need" — they are **not directed** by environmental pressures |
| **Direction** | Can be neutral, harmful, or beneficial (depending on environment) |
| **Contribution to evolution** | **Adds new genetic variation to a population** — the raw material for natural selection |

---

**② Genetic Drift**

- **Definition**: Random fluctuations in allele frequencies, especially significant in **small populations**
- **Mechanism**: Purely by "sampling error" → some alleles may disappear or become fixed by chance
- **Effect**: Reduces genetic diversity; causes **random divergence** between populations

| Type | Description | Consequences |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Bottleneck Effect** | Catastrophic event (volcanic eruption, fire, overhunting) **drastically reduces population to a few individuals** | Survivors' allele frequencies do not represent the original population → **severe loss of genetic diversity** |
| **Founder Effect** | A small group **separates** from the original population to establish a new population | The new population's allele frequencies **differ from** the source population → rare alleles may become common in the new population |

- **Classic case**: Northern elephant seals — hunted to ~20 individuals → now recovered to hundreds of thousands, but **genetic diversity is extremely low** (bottleneck effect)

---

**③ Gene Flow (Migration)**

- Movement of individuals into or out of a population → adds or removes alleles
- **Effects**:
  - Increases genetic diversity of the receiving population
  - **Reduces genetic differences between populations** → prevents divergence into separate species
  - Makes different populations' gene pools more similar

---

### LO 7.4.B: Describe the role of random processes in the evolution of specific populations.

- Mutation → produces new genetic variation → provides phenotypes for natural selection to act upon
- Genetic drift → small populations can randomly diverge from other populations of the same species → may lead to differentiation
- Gene flow between two populations → **prevents them from diverging into separate species**

### LO 7.4.C: Describe the change in the genetic makeup of a population over time.

- **Changes in allele frequencies provide evidence for the occurrence of evolution in a population**
- If $p$ or $q$ changes between generations → **evolution has occurred**

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.5 — Hardy–Weinberg Equilibrium

### LO 7.5.A: Describe the conditions under which allele and genotype frequencies will change in populations.

**HARDY-WEINBERG EQUILIBRIUM DEFINED**
A mathematical model for describing and predicting allele and genotype frequencies in a **non-evolving population**.

**FIVE CONDITIONS (Must ALL Be Met)**

| # | Condition | Consequence if Violated |
|---|-----------|------------------------|
| ① | **Large population size** | Genetic drift will occur |
| ② | **No migration (no gene flow)** | Gene flow changes allele frequencies |
| ③ | **No new mutations** | New alleles are introduced |
| ④ | **Random mating** | Non-random mating changes genotype frequencies |
| ⑤ | **No natural selection** | Some alleles are preferentially passed on |

> ⚠️ **These conditions are never fully met in nature**! Therefore, evolution is inevitable.
> **The true value of H-W equilibrium**: It provides a **null hypothesis** — if observed genotype frequencies significantly deviate from H-W expectations → evolution is occurring!

---

**CORE EQUATIONS AND HOW TO USE THEM**

**Equation 1 (Allele Frequencies)** :
$$p + q = 1$$

- $p$ = frequency of the dominant allele
- $q$ = frequency of the recessive allele

**Equation 2 (Genotype Frequencies)** :
$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$

- $p^2$ = frequency of homozygous dominant
- $2pq$ = frequency of heterozygotes
- $q^2$ = frequency of homozygous recessive

**STANDARD PROBLEM-SOLVING STEPS**

1. If you know the **recessive phenotype frequency** (= $q^2$) → take square root to get $q$
2. $p = 1 - q$
3. Calculate $p^2$ and $2pq$
4. Use **chi-square test** to compare observed vs. expected values → determine if significantly deviating from H-W equilibrium

### LO 7.6.B: Explain how morphological, biochemical, and geological data provide evidence that organisms have changed over time.

**THREE MAJOR CATEGORIES OF EVIDENCE — Detailed**

---

**① Fossil Evidence**

- **Fossil formation**: Organism remains buried by sediment → minerals replace organic material → petrification
- **Dating methods (3 types)** :
  1. **Relative age of rocks**: Based on geological strata (deeper layers are older)
  2. **Radiometric isotope dating**: Using known half-lives (e.g., carbon-14 half-life = 5,730 years)
  3. **Geographical data**: Plate tectonics, paleogeographic reconstruction
- **Transitional fossils**: Show intermediate forms between ancestral and descendant groups
  - Example: **Archaeopteryx** — transitional between reptiles and birds
  - Example: **Tiktaalik** — transitional between fish and tetrapods

---

**② Morphological Evidence**

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Homologous Structures** | Similar structures in different organisms **inherited from a common ancestor** (may have different functions) | Human arm, cat leg, whale flipper, bat wing — same bone arrangement |
| **Vestigial Structures** | Structures that **lost their original function** during evolution but are still retained | Human appendix, tailbone; whale pelvic bones; snake pelvic remnants |
| **Analogous Structures** | Similar function but **different evolutionary origin** (convergent evolution) | Bird wing vs. insect wing — NOT evidence of common ancestry |

> ⚠️ The framework requires understanding: **Homologous structures ≠ Analogous structures**. Only homologous structures provide evidence of common ancestry.

---

**③ Molecular Evidence**

- **DNA sequence comparison**: The more similar the nucleotide sequences shared by two species → the more closely related they are
- **Protein sequence comparison**: Comparing amino acid sequences of conserved proteins (e.g., cytochrome c, hemoglobin)
- **Molecular Clock**: Estimating divergence times based on the rate of accumulated mutations in DNA sequences

> ⚠️ The framework emphasizes: **Molecular data typically provide more accurate and reliable evidence than morphological traits**, because morphology can be influenced by convergent evolution.

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.9 — Phylogeny

### LO 7.9.A: Describe the types of evidence that can be used to infer an evolutionary relationship.

**PHYLOGENETIC TREES vs CLADOGRAMS**

| Feature | Phylogenetic Tree | Cladogram |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Shows time scale?** | **Yes** (branch length reflects evolutionary time or amount of change) | **No** |
| **Shows amount of evolutionary difference?** | **Yes** (branch length can represent genetic distance) | **No** |

| **Nature** | Quantitative model | Qualitative model |

---

**TERMINOLOGY**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Node** | Represents the **most recent common ancestor** of any two groups or lineages |
| **Branch** | Represents a lineage |
| **Out-group** | The lineage that is **least closely related** to the rest of the organisms in the tree/cladogram; used to determine the direction of evolutionary change |

**CONSTRUCTION BASES**

1. **Morphological similarities**: Comparing living and fossil species
2. **Molecular data**: DNA sequences, protein sequences
   - ⚠️ **Molecular data typically provide more accurate and reliable evidence than morphological traits** (because convergent evolution can create similar morphologies in unrelated species)

### LO 7.9.B: Explain how phylogenetic trees and cladograms can be used to infer evolutionary relatedness.

- Phylogenetic trees and cladograms are **hypotheses** that are **constantly being revised** based on new evidence
- The **more nodes two species share** on the tree → the more closely related they are → the **more recently** they diverged from a common ancestor
- Can be used to illustrate **speciation events** and their timing

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.10 — Speciation

### LO 7.10.A: Describe the conditions under which new species may arise.

**NECESSARY CONDITION FOR SPECIATION**
- Two populations become **reproductively isolated** from each other
- Gene flow is interrupted → populations evolve independently → genetic differences accumulate until they can no longer interbreed

**BIOLOGICAL SPECIES CONCEPT (BSC)**

- **Definition**: A species is a group of organisms capable of **interbreeding and producing viable, fertile offspring** under natural conditions
- **Limitations**:
  - Does **not** apply to **asexually reproducing** organisms (e.g., bacteria)
  - Does **not** apply to **fossils** (cannot test interbreeding)
  - Does **not** apply to **geographically isolated** populations (cannot test whether they would interbreed if they met)

---

### LO 7.10.B: Describe the rate of evolution and speciation under different ecological conditions.

**TEMPORAL PATTERNS OF EVOLUTION**

| Pattern | Description | Evidence |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Gradualism** | Evolution occurs through **small, incremental changes** accumulating over **millions of years** | Some fossil records show gradual morphological change |
| **Punctuated Equilibrium** | Species experience long periods of **stasis (no change)**, punctuated by **short periods of rapid evolution** | Fossil records often show "sudden" appearance of new species, followed by long periods of stability |

> ⚠️ These two patterns **are not mutually exclusive** — different species may follow different patterns.

---

**DIRECTIONAL PATTERNS OF EVOLUTION**

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Divergent Evolution** | Adaptation to **different habitats** leads to phenotypic diversification | Darwin's finches — beak shape diversification |
| **Adaptive Radiation** | When new habitats become available, one ancestral lineage rapidly diversifies into many species; **speciation rates are especially rapid** | Hawaiian Drosophila, Darwin's finches in the Galápagos |
| **Convergent Evolution** | **Similar selective pressures** lead to **similar phenotypic adaptations** in **unrelated** populations or species | Shark (cartilaginous fish) and dolphin (mammal) — both have streamlined body shapes |

---

### LO 7.10.C: Explain the processes and mechanisms that drive speciation.

**GEOGRAPHIC PATTERNS**

| Type | Description | Mechanism |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Allopatric Speciation** | Populations are separated by a **physical geographic barrier** (mountains, rivers, oceans) | Geographic isolation → gene flow interrupted → independent evolution → reproductive isolation |
| **Sympatric Speciation** | Populations have **geographic overlap** | Niche partitioning, polyploidy (common in plants), sexual selection, etc. |

---

**REPRODUCTIVE ISOLATING MECHANISMS**

**Pre-zygotic Isolation — Prevents Fertilization:**

| Mechanism | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Habitat Isolation** | Species in the same region occupy different habitats, never encounter each other | Two frog species — one breeds in ponds, the other in streams |
| **Temporal Isolation** | Breeding times are different | Two plant species — one flowers in spring, the other in summer |
| **Behavioral Isolation** | Courtship signals are different, not recognized | Different firefly species have different flash patterns |
| **Mechanical Isolation** | Reproductive structures are incompatible | Different snail species have differently oriented genital openings |
| **Gametic Isolation** | Gametes cannot fuse at the molecular level | Sperm and egg surface proteins do not match |

**Post-zygotic Isolation — Problems After Fertilization:**

| Mechanism | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Hybrid Inviability** | Hybrids cannot survive to reproductive maturity | Goat × sheep hybrids die as embryos |
| **Hybrid Sterility** | Hybrids survive but are sterile | Horse × donkey → **mule**: strong but sterile |
| **Hybrid Breakdown** | F₂ or backcross hybrids have reduced fitness | Some plant hybrids have weak second-generation offspring |

**💡 Illustrative Examples from the Framework**:
- **Hawaiian Drosophila**: Classic adaptive radiation — hundreds of species from a single ancestral species
- **Caribbean Anolis lizards**: Convergent evolution — similar body types evolving independently on different islands
- **Apple maggot (Rhagoletis)**: Sympatric speciation in progress — host race formation on hawthorn vs. introduced apple trees

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.11 — Variations in Populations

### LO 7.11.A: Explain how the genetic diversity of a species or population affects its ability to withstand environmental pressures.

**EVOLUTIONARY IMPORTANCE OF GENETIC DIVERSITY**

- **Affects population dynamics**: The level of variation directly determines a population's ability to respond to environmental change
- **Low diversity → High risk**: Species and populations with little genetic diversity face **greater risk of decline or extinction**
  - Reason: Insufficient diversity to respond to new selective pressures
  - **Inbreeding** → harmful recessive alleles become homozygous → reduced fitness
- **High diversity → Greater resilience**: More likely to contain **individuals that can withstand environmental pressure**

**ENVIRONMENT-DEPENDENT ALLELE FITNESS**
- Alleles that are **adaptive in one environment** may be **deleterious in another** due to different selective pressures
- Example: Sickle cell allele is advantageous in malaria-endemic regions, harmful in non-malarial regions

**💡 Illustrative Examples from the Framework**:
- **California condors**: Once reduced to ~20 individuals; severe inbreeding and extremely low genetic diversity
- **Black-footed ferrets**: Nearly extinct; all existing individuals descended from a very small number of ancestors
- **Prairie chickens**: Illinois population — reduced genetic diversity led to decreased hatching success
- **Potato blight**: Monoculture (genetically uniform) → pathogen spreads rapidly → Irish Potato Famine
- **Corn rust**: Similar monoculture vulnerability
- **Antibiotic resistance in bacteria**: Not all individuals are susceptible to antibiotics → diverse populations are more likely to contain resistant individuals

## 🌿 TOPIC 7.12 — Origins of Life on Earth

### LO 7.12.A: Describe the scientific evidence that supports models of the origin of life on Earth.

**GEOLOGICAL TIMELINE**

| Event | Time | Explanation |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Earth formed** | ~**4.6 billion years ago (bya)** | |
| **Environment became capable of supporting life** | ~**3.9 bya** | Prior to this, the environment was too hostile (frequent meteor impacts, high temperatures, etc.) |
| **Earliest fossil evidence** | ~**3.5 bya** | Stromatolites — fossilized microbial mats |
| **Window for origin of life** | **3.9 bya – 3.5 bya** | Approximately 400 million years |

> ⚠️ **Evolution ≠ Origin of Life**: Evolution explains how life diversified once it existed, but it does **not** explain how life originally arose. These are two separate scientific questions.

---

**THE RNA WORLD HYPOTHESIS**

This is currently the most widely accepted model for the origin of life.

**Three Core Assumptions:**

1. **RNA as the earliest genetic material**
   - Before DNA and proteins evolved, RNA both carried genetic information and had catalytic functions
   - RNA can self-replicate (though inefficiently) — ensuring **genetic continuity**

2. **Base pairing is necessary for replication**
   - RNA strands can undergo template-directed synthesis through complementary base pairing
   - This is the simplest form of information copying

3. **Genetically encoded proteins were not involved as catalysts**
   - In the earliest life forms, **ribozymes (catalytic RNA)** performed the functions later carried out by protein enzymes
   - Protein enzymes were a later evolutionary "upgrade"

**Evidence Supporting the RNA World Hypothesis:**
- **rRNA in the ribosome is the peptidyl transferase** — it catalyzes peptide bond formation; it is a **ribozyme**
- RNA can be abiotically synthesized from simple precursors (nucleotides) under laboratory conditions
- The **Miller-Urey experiment** (1953) demonstrated that organic molecules (including amino acids and nucleotide bases) can form spontaneously under conditions simulating early Earth

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.1 — Responses to the Environment

### LO 8.1.A: Explain how the behavioral and physiological response of an organism is related to changes in internal or external environment.

- Organisms respond to changes in their environment through **behavioral mechanisms** and **physiological mechanisms**
- **Internal changes**: Body temperature, blood glucose, hormone levels, etc.
- **External cues**: Light, temperature, humidity, predators, food availability, etc.
- Organisms exchange information with one another in response to internal changes and external cues → behavior changes

**💡 Examples (Behavioral Responses)** :
- **Photoperiodism in plants**: Plants regulate flowering time based on day length
- **Phototropism in plants**: Plants grow toward light
- **Taxis in animals**: **Directed** movement toward or away from a stimulus (e.g., moths flying toward light)
- **Kinesis in animals**: **Non-directed** movement rate changes with stimulus intensity (e.g., pill bugs move faster in dry conditions)
- **Nocturnal and diurnal activity**: Adjusting activity patterns based on light cycles
- **Fight-or-flight response**: Physiological response to threats (epinephrine release, increased heart rate, etc.)

**💡 Examples (Information Exchange)** :
- **Predator warnings** (e.g., alarm calls in birds)
- **Plant responses to herbivory**: Release of volatile chemicals attracting predatory wasps

> ❌ **Exclusion**: Knowledge of specific behavioral or physiological mechanisms is beyond the scope of the AP Exam.

### LO 8.1.B: Explain how the behavioral responses of organisms affect their overall fitness and may contribute to the success of a population.

**MECHANISMS OF COMMUNICATION (5 Types Listed in the Framework)**

| Communication Type | Description | Example |
|-------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Visual signals** | Color, movement, posture | Peacock display, honeybee dance |
| **Audible signals** | Sound | Bird songs, wolf howls, frog calls |
| **Tactile signals** | Physical contact | Bee antennae communication, primate grooming |
| **Electrical signals** | Electric fields | Certain fish (electric eels) use electrical signals |
| **Chemical signals** | Pheromones, chemical cues | Ant trail pheromones, mammalian territorial marking |

---

**BEHAVIOR AND FITNESS**

- Signaling behaviors produce changes in the behavior of other organisms and can result in **differential reproductive success**
  - More effective courtship signals → more mating opportunities → more offspring
  - More effective territory defense → more resources → higher reproductive success
- **Responses to information and communication of information** are **vital to natural selection and evolution**

**INNATE vs LEARNED BEHAVIORS**
- **Innate Behavior**: Present from birth, does not depend on experience (e.g., spider web spinning, sea turtle returning to birthplace)
- **Learned Behavior**: Acquired through experience (e.g., birds learning to identify predators)

**Fitness favors** behaviors (whether innate or learned) that increase survival and reproductive success.

---

**COOPERATIVE BEHAVIOR**

- Tends to increase the **fitness of the individual** and the **survival of the population**
- Seemingly "altruistic" behaviors often increase the individual's **inclusive fitness** from an evolutionary perspective
- **Kin Selection**: By helping close relatives reproduce, an individual indirectly passes on its own genes

**💡 Examples**:
- **Parent-offspring interactions**: Parental care → increased offspring survival
- **Courtship and mating behaviors**: Ensuring successful reproduction
- **Foraging by bees and other animals**: Social insects with division of labor
- **Pack behavior in animals**: Wolves — coordinated hunting increases success
- **Herd, flock, and schooling behavior**: Reduced predation risk (safety in numbers)
- **Predator warnings**: Alerting conspecifics to danger
- **Colony and swarming behavior in insects**: Ant colonies, bee hives
- **Kin selection**: Worker bees are sterile but help the queen reproduce (protecting their sisters' genes)

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.2 — Energy Flow Through Ecosystems

### LO 8.2.A: Describe the strategies organisms use to acquire and use energy.

**BASIC USES OF ENERGY**
Organisms use energy to: **maintain organization, grow, reproduce, and maintain homeostasis**.

---

**ENDOTHERMS vs ECTOTHERMS**

| Feature | Endotherms | Ectotherms |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **Heat source** | Primarily **metabolic heat production** (internal) | Primarily from **external environment** (sunlight, ground, etc.) |
| **Temperature regulation efficiency** | High (maintain constant body temperature) | Low (body temperature fluctuates with environment) |
| **Energy requirement** | **High** (need lots of food to maintain body temperature) | **Low** (do not need to produce metabolic heat) |
| **Examples** | Mammals, birds | Reptiles, amphibians, fish, insects |
| **Behavioral regulation** | Less reliance (mainly physiological regulation) | Often use behavioral regulation (basking in sun, seeking shade, aggregating) |

---

**ENERGY BALANCE AND ORGANISMAL HEALTH**

| State | Result |
|-------|--------|
| **Net energy gain** | Energy storage (e.g., fat) → growth → increased reproductive output |
| **Net energy loss** | Loss of mass → decreased reproductive output → eventually death |

**ENERGY AVAILABILITY AND REPRODUCTIVE STRATEGIES**
- Different organisms use various reproductive strategies in response to energy availability
- Some organisms **alternate between asexual and sexual reproduction** in response to energy availability:
  - Example: **Daphnia (water fleas)** — reproduce asexually in favorable conditions, switch to sexual reproduction when conditions deteriorate
  - Example: **Yeast** — bud asexually when nutrients are abundant; switch to sexual reproduction under nutrient stress

**💡 Framework Examples**:
- **Seasonal reproduction in animals and plants**: Many species reproduce during the season when energy is most abundant (e.g., spring)
- **Life-history strategies**: Biennial plants (store energy in first year, flower and fruit in second year); reproductive diapause (pause reproduction when conditions are unfavorable)

---

### LO 8.2.B: Explain how energy flows and matter cycles through trophic levels.

**ECOLOGICAL LEVELS OF ORGANIZATION (Smallest to Largest)**

```
Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere
```

---

**ENERGY FLOW vs MATTER CYCLING — Core Distinction**

| Dimension | Energy Flow | Matter Cycling |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Direction** | **One-way** (sun → producers → consumers → heat loss) | **Cyclic** (moves continuously between environment and organisms) |
| **Can it be recycled?** | **Cannot be recycled** — eventually all dissipates as heat | **Can be recycled** — matter is conserved, continuously reused |
| **Driven by** | Requires continuous **external energy input** (primarily the sun) | Driven by energy flow |

---

**THREE MAJOR BIOGEOCHEMICAL CYCLES — Detailed Descriptions**

---

**① Water Cycle (Hydrologic Cycle)**

- **Reservoirs**: Oceans (largest), surface water (lakes, rivers), groundwater, atmosphere (water vapor), glaciers, living organisms
- **Processes**:
  - **Evaporation**: From oceans/surface water → water vapor enters the atmosphere
  - **Transpiration**: Water vapor released from plant leaf stomata
  - **Condensation**: Water vapor cools → forms clouds
  - **Precipitation**: Rain, snow, hail, etc.
  - **Runoff & Infiltration**: Water returns to oceans and groundwater

---

**② Carbon Cycle**

- **Key reactions**:
  - **Photosynthesis**: CO₂ + H₂O → organic compounds (carbohydrates) + O₂
  - **Cellular Respiration**: Organic compounds + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy
  - **Decomposition**: Microorganisms break down dead organisms → release CO₂
  - **Combustion**: Burning of fossil fuels → releases CO₂ (major human impact)
- **Carbon reservoirs**: Atmosphere (CO₂), fossil fuels, ocean (dissolved carbonates), biomass (organic matter), rocks (carbonate rocks)
- ⚠️ **Human activity through burning fossil fuels dramatically increases atmospheric CO₂ → global climate change**

---

**③ Nitrogen Cycle — The Most Complex Cycle**

| Step | Process | Conversion | Microorganisms Involved |
|------|---------|------------|------------------------|
| **① Nitrogen Fixation** | Atmospheric N₂ converted to ammonia (NH₃) | N₂ → NH₃ | Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., **Rhizobium** symbiotic with legumes) |
| **② Assimilation** | Plants absorb NH₄⁺ or NO₃⁻ to synthesize proteins/nucleic acids | Inorganic N → Organic N | Not required (done by plants themselves) |
| **③ Ammonification** | Decomposition of organic N wastes → releases NH₄⁺ | Organic N → NH₄⁺ | Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) |
| **④ Nitrification** | NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ | Ammonium → Nitrite → Nitrate | Nitrifying bacteria (*Nitrosomonas*, *Nitrobacter*) |
| **⑤ Denitrification** | NO₃⁻ → N₂ (returns to atmosphere) | Nitrate → Nitrogen gas | Denitrifying bacteria |

- **Largest nitrogen reservoir**: **Atmosphere (78% N₂)** — but most organisms **cannot directly use N₂**!
- Key limitation: Nitrogen fixation rate often limits ecosystem productivity

---

**④ Phosphorus Cycle — Unique: No Gas Phase**

- **The only biogeochemical cycle with no atmospheric component**
- **Reservoir**: Rocks and sediments (phosphate minerals)
- **Process**:
  1. **Weathering**: Rocks break down → release PO₄³⁻ into soil and groundwater
  2. **Uptake**: Producers absorb phosphate → incorporated into ATP, DNA, RNA, phospholipids, etc.
  3. **Food chain transfer**: Consumers eat producers → phosphorus moves through trophic levels
  4. **Return to soil**: Decomposition of biomass and excretion
  5. **Sedimentation**: Some phosphate runs off into oceans → settles to seafloor → returns as rock over geological timescales
- **Limiting factor**: In freshwater ecosystems, phosphorus is often the **most limiting nutrient for primary productivity** (along with nitrogen)

---

### LO 8.2.C: Explain how changes in energy availability affect populations, communities, and ecosystems.

- **Changes in sunlight** → affects primary productivity → affects all trophic levels
- **Changes in the biomass or number of producers** in a given geographic area → cascading effects throughout the food web

**COMPLETE TROPHIC LEVEL STRUCTURE**

```
Trophic Level 4:    Quaternary Consumers (top predators)
                        ↑
Trophic Level 3:    Tertiary Consumers
                        ↑
Trophic Level 2:    Secondary Consumers
                        ↑
Trophic Level 1:    Primary Consumers (herbivores)
                        ↑
Trophic Level 0:    Producers (autotrophs)
                        ↑
                  (Sunlight / Inorganic Chemical Energy)
```

- **Decomposers**: Work at all trophic levels, breaking down dead organic matter and releasing nutrients
- **Energy transfer efficiency**: Approximately **10%** passes from one trophic level to the next (the rest is lost as heat)
  - This explains why **trophic levels rarely exceed 4-5**

---

### LO 8.2.D: Explain how the activities of autotrophs and heterotrophs enable the flow of energy within an ecosystem.

**AUTOTROPHS (Self-feeders)**

Organisms that can capture energy from **inorganic sources**.

| Type | Energy Source | Carbon Source | Examples |
|------|---------------|---------------|----------|
| **Photoautotrophs** | **Sunlight** | CO₂ | Plants, algae, cyanobacteria |
| **Chemoautotrophs** | **Inorganic chemical reactions** (e.g., H₂S, NH₃, Fe²⁺) | CO₂ | Deep-sea vent bacteria, nitrifying bacteria |

- **Photoautotrophs** → basis of **Primary Productivity**
- **Chemoautotrophs** → support **lightless ecosystems** (e.g., deep-sea hydrothermal vents)

---

**HETEROTROPHS (Other-feeders)**

Must obtain energy from **organic matter**.

| Type | Food Source | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Herbivores** | Eat producers directly | Cows, deer, caterpillars |
| **Carnivores** | Eat other consumers | Lions, eagles, snakes |
| **Omnivores** | Eat both plants and animals | Humans, bears, crows |
| **Decomposers** | Break down dead organic matter | Bacteria, fungi |
| **Scavengers** | Eat already dead organisms | Vultures, earthworms |

- Heterotrophs **metabolize carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins** as energy sources
- They capture the energy present in carbon compounds by **consuming organic matter** derived from autotrophs

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.3 — Population Ecology

### LO 8.3.A: Describe factors that influence growth dynamics of populations.

**DEFINITION OF POPULATION**
- Individuals of the **same species** that interact with one another and with the environment, in the **same area at the same time**

**POPULATION GROWTH DYNAMICS**
Population growth depends on three basic parameters:
- **Birth Rate (B)**: Number of new individuals produced per unit time
- **Death Rate (D)**: Number of individuals that die per unit time
- **Current Population Size (N)**

**POPULATION GROWTH EQUATION**

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = B - D$$

Where:
- $dN$ = change in population size
- $dt$ = change in time
- $B$ = birth rate
- $D$ = death rate
- $N$ = population size

**EXPONENTIAL GROWTH**

- **Condition**: Resources are **unlimited**, reproduction is **unconstrained**
- **J-shaped curve**
- **Formula**: $\frac{dN}{dt} = r_{max}N$
- $r_{max}$ = **maximum per capita growth rate** of the population
- In reality, only occurs under special circumstances for limited periods (e.g., bacteria in a fresh culture, invasive species in a new environment)

---

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.4 — Effect of Density on Populations

### LO 8.4.A: Explain how the density of a population affects and is determined by resource availability in the environment.

**CARRYING CAPACITY (K)**

- **Definition**: The sustainable abundance of a species that can be supported by the ecosystem's **total available resources**
- **Not fixed** — changes with environmental conditions and resource availability

---

**DENSITY-DEPENDENT FACTORS**

| Factor | Mechanism | Effect Increases with Density |
|--------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Competition** | Individuals compete for limited resources (food, space, mates) | **Intensifies** |
| **Predation** | Predators concentrate in high-density areas | Predation pressure **increases** |
| **Disease** | High density facilitates pathogen transmission | Transmission rate **increases** |
| **Waste accumulation** | Build-up of metabolic waste (e.g., ammonia) | Toxic effects **increase** |

**DENSITY-INDEPENDENT FACTORS**
- Effect is **unrelated to population density**
- Examples: Natural disasters (fires, floods, hurricanes), extreme weather, volcanic eruptions, pollution events

---

**LOGISTIC GROWTH**

- **Condition**: Resources are limited → growth is constrained
- **S-shaped (sigmoidal) curve**
- **Formula**: $\frac{dN}{dt} = r_{max}N\frac{K-N}{K}$
- When $N$ is much smaller than $K$: $(K-N)/K \approx 1$ → approximates exponential growth
- When $N$ approaches $K$: $(K-N)/K \approx 0$ → growth rate approaches zero
- When $N$ exceeds $K$: $(K-N)/K$ becomes negative → population declines (overshoot and die-off)

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.5 — Community Ecology

### LO 8.5.A: Describe the structure of a community according to its species composition and diversity.

- **Community** = All populations of **different species** living and interacting in the same area
- **Species composition**: Which species are present in the community
- **Species diversity**: Includes both **species richness** (number of species) and **species evenness** (how evenly individuals are distributed among species)

**SIMPSON'S DIVERSITY INDEX**

$$Diversity\ Index = 1 - \sum\left(\frac{n}{N}\right)^2$$

Where:
- $n$ = total number of organisms of a particular species
- $N$ = total number of organisms of all species

**Interpretation**:
- Index range: 0 ~ 1
- **Value closer to 1** → higher diversity (high species richness, good evenness)
- **Value closer to 0** → lower diversity (a few species dominate)

---

### LO 8.5.B: Explain how interactions within and among populations influence community structure.

**TYPES OF INTERSPECIFIC INTERACTIONS**

| Interaction | Species 1 | Species 2 | Description |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Predation** | + | – | One organism (predator) eats another (prey) |
| **Competition** | – | – | Two organisms compete for the same limited resource |
| **Mutualism** | + | + | Both benefit |
| **Commensalism** | + | 0 | One benefits, the other is unaffected |
| **Parasitism** | + | – | One organism (parasite) lives in/on another (host), obtaining nutrients |
| **Cooperation** | + | + | Mutual benefit among individuals of the **same species** |

---

**ADVANCED COMMUNITY ECOLOGY CONCEPTS (Framework Required)**

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Predator-Prey Dynamics** | Classic oscillating population cycles between predator and prey (Lotka-Volterra model) |
| **Trophic Cascade** | Top predators indirectly affect lower trophic levels by controlling intermediate consumers |
| **Niche Partitioning** | Species reduce direct competition by dividing resources (differences in habitat, food type, time, etc.) |
| **Keystone Species** | A species whose effect on community structure is **disproportionately large** relative to its abundance |

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.6 — Biodiversity

### LO 8.6.A: Describe the relationship between ecosystem diversity and its resilience to changes in the environment.

**DIVERSITY-STABILITY HYPOTHESIS**

- **Ecosystems with fewer component parts and less diversity** → **less resilient** → more vulnerable to environmental change
  - Example: **Monoculture farm** → one disease can destroy the entire crop
- **Ecosystems with more components and higher diversity** → **more resilient** → better able to withstand and recover from disturbances
  - Example: **Tropical rainforest** → rich species diversity creates complex ecological networks with **functional redundancy**

---

### LO 8.6.B: Explain how the addition or removal of any component of an ecosystem will affect its overall short-term and long-term structure.

**THE UNIQUE ROLE OF KEYSTONE SPECIES**

- **Definition**: A species whose effect on the ecosystem is **disproportionate** relative to its abundance
- **Key feature**: When keystone species are removed from the ecosystem, the ecosystem **often collapses**
- **Classic examples**:
  - **Sea otter**: Preys on sea urchins → protects kelp forests → maintains the entire ecosystem
  - **Gray wolf**: Preys on elk → prevents overgrazing → maintains vegetation and stream ecosystems
  - **Bees and other pollinators**: Pollination services → supports reproduction of many flowering plants

**CHAIN OF EFFECTS WHEN A KEYSTONE SPECIES IS REMOVED**

```
Keystone species removed
    ↓
Prey species population explodes
    ↓
Overconsumption of resources
    ↓
Other species lose habitat/food
    ↓
Ecosystem collapses or undergoes dramatic shift
```

## 🌍 TOPIC 8.7 — Disruptions in Ecosystems

### LO 8.7.A: Explain the interaction between the environment and random or preexisting variations in populations.

**DEFINITION OF ADAPTATION**
- A **genetic variation** that is **favored by selection** and manifests as a trait that provides an **advantage** to an organism in a particular environment
- ⚠️ **Adaptations do NOT arise "for" a specific purpose** — they are simply retained because they increased fitness
- ⚠️ **Mutations are NOT directed by environmental pressures** — mutations are random; the environment cannot "instruct" organisms to produce specific mutations

**HETEROZYGOTE ADVANTAGE**

- When the **heterozygous genotype** has **higher relative fitness** than either homozygous dominant or homozygous recessive genotype
- **Classic case: Sickle cell anemia**:
  - Hb^A Hb^A (normal): susceptible to malaria in malaria-endemic regions
  - Hb^S Hb^S (sickle cell): severe anemia, low fitness
  - **Hb^A Hb^S (heterozygous)**: mild anemia but **resistant to malaria** → **highest fitness in malaria-endemic regions**

---

### LO 8.7.B: Explain how invasive species affect ecosystem dynamics.

**CHARACTERISTICS OF INVASIVE SPECIES**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Introduction** | **Intentional or unintentional** (introduced by humans to a region outside their native range) |
| **Competitive advantage** | New environment lacks **natural predators, competitors, and parasites** → less energy spent on defense |
| **Reproductive capacity** | Usually reproduce quickly and produce large numbers of offspring |
| **Niche use** | May occupy **empty niches** or **directly outcompete native species** for resources |
| **Consequences** | Reduce native biodiversity, alter ecosystem structure, cause economic damage |

**💡 Classic Examples**:

| Invasive Species | Origin | Introduced To | Impact |
|------------------|--------|---------------|--------|
| **Kudzu** | Japan/China | Southeastern USA | Rapidly overgrows, smothers native vegetation |
| **Zebra mussels** | Caspian Sea | North American Great Lakes | Clog pipes, outcompete native mussels |
| **Feral pigs** | Eurasia | Worldwide | Destroy vegetation and soil |
| **Burmese python** | Southeast Asia | Florida Everglades | Nearly eliminated local small mammals |

### LO 8.7.C: Describe human activities that lead to changes in ecosystem structure and dynamics.

**HUMAN-ACCELERATED ENVIRONMENTAL CHANGES**

| Activity/Phenomenon | Description |
|---------------------|-------------|
| **Biomagnification** | Toxic substances (e.g., DDT, mercury) become **increasingly concentrated at higher trophic levels** → top predators have the highest concentrations |
| **Eutrophication** | Excess nitrogen and phosphorus (from agricultural fertilizers, sewage) enter water bodies → **algal bloom** → algae die and decompose → oxygen depletion → **fish kills** |
| **Global climate change** | Burning fossil fuels → increased CO₂ → greenhouse effect → global warming, extreme weather events |
| **Logging** | Forest destruction → biodiversity loss, carbon sink reduction, soil erosion |
| **Urbanization** | Habitat fragmentation and isolation → populations become isolated → loss of genetic diversity |
| **Monocropping** | Genetic uniformity → vulnerability to disease/pest outbreaks (e.g., Irish Potato Famine) |
| **Overfishing/overhunting** | Directly drives species toward endangerment or extinction |

**NATURAL DISTURBANCES (Also Listed in the Framework)**

| Phenomenon | Impact |
|------------|--------|
| **El Niño** | Abnormal Pacific Ocean surface temperatures → global climate anomalies → ecosystem effects |
| **Continental drift** | Changes species distribution and isolation patterns over geological timescales |
| **Meteor impact** | End-Cretaceous (65 million years ago) meteor impact → dinosaur extinction → opened niches for mammals |
