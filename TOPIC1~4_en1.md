## TOPIC 1.1 — Structure of Water and Hydrogen Bonding

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 4 (Systems Interactions)

### 📌 LO 1.1.A
Explain how the properties of water resulting from its polarity and hydrogen bonding affect its biological functions.

---

#### 📍 EK 1.1.A.1 — Living systems depend on properties of water to survive

**(i) Water's Polarity arises from polar covalent bonds**

**Full Explanation:**
- A water molecule (H₂O) consists of 1 oxygen atom covalently bonded to 2 hydrogen atoms
- Oxygen's **electronegativity (3.44)** is much greater than hydrogen's (2.20) → **electrons in the covalent bond are unevenly attracted** toward the oxygen atom
- Result: Oxygen acquires a **partial negative charge** (δ⁻), each hydrogen acquires a **partial positive charge** (δ⁺)
- This charge distribution makes water a **polar molecule** — having a positive and negative end
- Due to water's polarity, **hydrogen bonds** form between adjacent water molecules: the δ⁺ hydrogen of one water molecule is electrostatically attracted to the δ⁻ oxygen of another
- **Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds** (2 donated via hydrogens, 2 accepted via oxygen)
- Hydrogen bonds are weak interactions (~20 times weaker than covalent bonds), but the collective effect of many hydrogen bonds is extremely significant
- Hydrogen bonds exist not only between water molecules but also within/between biological macromolecules (e.g., base pairing in DNA double strands, secondary structure in protein folding)

**(ii) High Specific Heat Capacity**

**Full Explanation:**
- **Definition**: The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C
- Water's specific heat capacity is **4.184 J/(g·°C)** — the highest among common liquids (second only to ammonia)
- **Reason**: To raise the temperature of water molecules, the extensive hydrogen bond network must first be broken, which requires significant energy
- **Biological significance**:
  - **Body temperature maintenance**: The human body is ~60-70% water. When external temperature changes, the water in the body absorbs or releases large amounts of heat while undergoing minimal temperature change itself
  - **Thermal buffering**: Large bodies of water (oceans, lakes) change temperature much more slowly than land, providing a stable temperature environment for aquatic organisms
  - **Temperature stability within organisms**: Even when cellular metabolism produces heat, water's specific heat capacity prevents drastic temperature increases that would denature proteins

**(iii) High Heat of Vaporization**

**Full Explanation:**
- **Definition**: The amount of heat required to completely convert 1 gram of liquid into gas at its boiling point
- Water's heat of vaporization at 100°C is **2260 J/g**, the highest among common liquids
- **Reason**: Hydrogen bonds between water molecules must be broken during evaporation
- **Biological significance**:
  - **Evaporative Cooling**: When water evaporates from a surface, it carries away large amounts of heat, cooling the remaining surface
  - **Sweating**: Mammals secrete sweat through sweat glands; evaporation of sweat carries away large amounts of body heat
  - **Panting**: Certain animals (dogs) cool down by rapid breathing that evaporates moisture from the tongue and respiratory tract
  - **Transpiration**: Evaporation of water from stomata in plant leaves produces a cooling effect and drives water transport upward from roots

---

#### 📍 EK 1.1.A.2 — Hydrogen bonds lead to cohesion, adhesion, and surface tension

**Full Explanation:**
- **Cohesion**:
  - Definition: Mutual attraction between like molecules
  - Water's cohesion arises from hydrogen bonds between adjacent water molecules
  - **Biological significance**: In plant xylem vessels, water molecules form a continuous **water column** via cohesion, extending from roots to leaves. When leaves lose water through transpiration, cohesion pulls the entire column upward (**Transpiration-Cohesion Theory**)

- **Adhesion**:
  - Definition: Attraction between different molecules
  - Water molecules adhere via hydrogen bonds to **polar cell wall materials (e.g., cellulose, lignin)**
  - **Biological significance**: In plant vessels, adhesion helps water resist gravity's downward pull, allowing water to climb along vessel walls. Adhesion + cohesion work together against gravity

- **Surface Tension**:
  - Definition: The property of liquid surface molecules being pulled downward by intermolecular forces (since attraction from air molecules above is much weaker than from liquid molecules below), forming an "elastic film" characteristic
  - Water has the **highest surface tension** of all common liquids (except mercury)
  - **Biological significance**:
    - Insects like **water striders** can walk on water without sinking
    - Some aquatic invertebrates use surface tension to forage on water surfaces
    - Water droplets on plant leaves maintain a spherical shape

## TOPIC 1.3 — Introduction to Macromolecules

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 4 (Systems Interactions)

### 📌 LO 1.3.A
Describe the chemical reactions that build and break down biological macromolecules.

#### 📍 EK 1.3.A.1 — Hydrolysis — Breakdown

**Full Explanation:**
- **Etymology**: "Hydro" = water, "lysis" = splitting/destruction
- **Essence**: In polymers, **adding one water molecule** to **break the covalent bond** between two monomers
- **Specific mechanism**:
  - The **H⁺** (hydrogen ion/proton) from water (H₂O) is added to one monomer
  - The remaining **OH⁻** (hydroxyl group) is added to the other monomer
  - Result: one covalent bond is broken, two monomers separate
- **Energy characteristic**: Typically **exergonic** (releases energy), but requires enzyme catalysis to lower activation energy
- **Biological examples**:
  - **Digestion**: Amylase hydrolyzes starch (polysaccharide) into maltose/glucose; proteases hydrolyze proteins into amino acids; lipases hydrolyze fats into glycerol and fatty acids
  - **Intracellular**: Macromolecule degradation and recycling, e.g., hydrolytic enzymes in lysosomes digest aging organelles
- **Comparison with dehydration synthesis**: Hydrolysis is the **reverse reaction** of dehydration synthesis

#### 📍 EK 1.3.A.2 — Dehydration Synthesis — Building

**Full Explanation:**
- **Alternative name**: Condensation reaction
- **Essence**: Two smaller molecules connect via a covalent bond, simultaneously **losing one water molecule**
- **Specific mechanism (chemical equation)**:
  ```
  Monomer₁-OH + H-Monomer₂ → Monomer₁-Monomer₂ + H₂O
  ```
  - Remove one **H⁺** from one monomer
  - Remove one **OH⁻** from the other monomer
  - H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form H₂O
  - The two monomers are connected by the remaining **covalent bond**
- **Energy characteristic**: Typically **endergonic** (requires energy input)
- **Polymerization**: Multiple monomers join through repeated dehydration synthesis to form long chains
- **Biological examples**:
  - **Polysaccharide synthesis**: Monosaccharides → starch/glycogen/cellulose
  - **Protein synthesis**: Amino acids → polypeptides (peptide bond formation)
  - **Nucleic acid synthesis**: Nucleotides → DNA/RNA chains (phosphodiester bond formation)
- **Reverse reaction**: Hydrolysis

**Comparison Table of the Two Reactions:**
| Feature | Dehydration Synthesis | Hydrolysis |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| Direction | Small → Large molecules | Large → Small molecules |
| Role of Water | **Releases** water | **Consumes** water |
| Energy Requirement | **Requires** ATP energy | **Releases** energy |
| When it occurs | When cells synthesize macromolecules | During digestion/degradation |
| Enzyme type | Synthetases | Hydrolases |

## TOPIC 1.4 — Carbohydrates

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 4 (Systems Interactions)

### 📌 LO 1.4.A
Describe the structure and function of carbohydrates.

#### 📍 EK 1.4.A.1 — Monosaccharides are the monomers of polysaccharides

**Full Explanation:**

**(1) Monosaccharides** — The "basic building blocks" of carbohydrates
- General molecular formula: **(CH₂O)ₙ**, where n = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7...
- Classification by number of carbon atoms:
  - Triose (C₃H₆O₃): e.g., glyceraldehyde
  - Pentose (C₅H₁₀O₅): **ribose** and **deoxyribose** — constitute nucleic acids
  - Hexose (C₆H₁₂O₆): **glucose**, **fructose**, **galactose**
- Monosaccharides are **water-soluble** and sweet-tasting
- They are the main fuel for cellular **respiration**
- Structural features: contain carbonyl group (C=O) and multiple hydroxyl groups (-OH)

**(2) Disaccharides** — Two monosaccharides joined by dehydration synthesis
- **Maltose**: glucose + glucose
- **Sucrose**: glucose + fructose (most common food sugar)
- **Lactose**: glucose + galactose (found in milk)

**(3) Polysaccharides** — Polymers of many monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds
- Can be **linear** or **branched**
- Function depends on monomer linkage pattern and degree of branching

**Three Specific Polysaccharides Required by the Curriculum:**
| Polysaccharide | Structural Features | Branching | Function | Distribution |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Starch** | α-glucose, α-1,4- and α-1,6-glycosidic bonds | Slightly branched (amylopectin) or linear (amylose) | **Energy storage** | Plant cells (chloroplasts/plastids) |
| **Glycogen** | α-glucose, highly α-1,4- and α-1,6-glycosidic bonds | **Highly branched** | **Energy storage** | Animal cells (liver, muscle) |
| **Cellulose** | β-glucose, β-1,4-glycosidic bonds | **Unbranched, linear** | **Structural support** | Main component of plant cell walls |

**Key Distinction — Starch vs. Cellulose:**
- Both are polymers composed of glucose monomers
- But the **type of glycosidic bond differs** (α vs β linkage) → completely different structures
- The β-1,4 bond in cellulose makes chains linear, with extensive hydrogen bonds between chains forming microfibrils
- Humans can digest starch (have α-amylase) but cannot digest cellulose (lack β-cellulase)
- **Ruminants** (cows, sheep) have microorganisms in their gut that can break down cellulose

## TOPIC 1.5 — Lipids

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 4 (Systems Interactions)

### 📌 LO 1.5.A
Describe the structure and function of lipids.

#### 📍 EK 1.5.A.1 — The nonpolar hydrophobic property of lipids and saturated/unsaturated fatty acids

**Full Explanation:**

**Defining characteristics of lipids:**
- **Nonpolar** — insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents (e.g., ether, chloroform)
- **Hydrophobic** — "water-fearing," due to the lack of charged or polar groups in the molecule
- Lipids are not polymers (unlike the other three major macromolecules), but are assembled from **different subcomponents**

**Saturated Fatty Acids:**

**Structural features (must master):**
- Only **single bonds** between all carbon atoms in the carbon chain
- Each carbon atom is "saturated" with hydrogen atoms (general formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH)
  ```
  e.g., CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-...-COOH (all single bonds, straight chain)
  ```
- Molecules are **linear**, allowing tight packing

**Physical properties:**
- Strong intermolecular van der Waals forces (due to tight packing)
- **Solid at room temperature** (e.g., butter, lard, tallow)

**Biological significance:**
- Animal fats are mainly composed of saturated fatty acids
- Dense packing → stores more energy per unit mass

**Unsaturated Fatty Acids:**

**Structural features (must master):**
- **At least one double bond** in the carbon chain
- The presence of a double bond causes a **kink/bend** in the carbon chain at that position
  ```
  e.g., CH₃-CH₂-CH=CH-CH₂-...-COOH (double bond causes bending)
  ```
- Double bonds can be:
  - **Cis**: hydrogen atoms on the same side of the double bond → natural fatty acids are mainly cis → greater bending
  - **Trans**: hydrogen atoms on opposite sides → no bending (trans fats)

**Physical properties:**
- Molecules cannot pack tightly (due to bending)
- More double bonds → more bending → weaker intermolecular forces
- **Liquid at room temperature** (e.g., olive oil, corn oil, canola oil)
- **Rule summary**: Higher unsaturation → more liquid the fat

**Biological significance:**
- Plant fats are typically unsaturated (liquid oils)
- Maintains cell membrane fluidity — more unsaturated fatty acids in membrane lipids → more "fluid" membrane

---

#### 📍 EK 1.5.A.2 — Diverse functions of lipids

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Fats — Energy storage and insulation**
- **Chemical structure**: 1 glycerol + 3 fatty acids = triglyceride
- **Energy storage**:
  - Each gram of fat releases ~**9 kcal** of energy, **more than double** that of carbohydrates (~4 kcal/g)
  - Primary long-term energy storage form in animals (adipose tissue)
- **Insulation**:
  - Subcutaneous fat layer **reduces heat loss**, helping mammals maintain body temperature
  - Polar animals (whales, seals) have thick blubber layers
- **Mechanical protection**: Fat pads provide physical cushioning around vital organs like kidneys

**(ii) Steroids — Hormonal signaling**
- **Chemical structure**: Four fused carbon rings (three six-carbon rings + one five-carbon ring)
- As hormones, regulate various physiological functions:
  - **Testosterone**: Male characteristic development
  - **Estrogen**: Female characteristic development and reproductive cycle
  - **Cortisol**: Stress response and glucose metabolism
  - **Cholesterol**: Precursor to all steroid hormones

**(iii) Cholesterol — Membrane stability**
- **Embedded in the phospholipid bilayer** of animal cell membranes
- Regulates membrane **fluidity**:
  - At high temperatures: cholesterol restricts phospholipid movement → reduces fluidity
  - At low temperatures: cholesterol prevents tight packing of phospholipids → prevents membrane solidification (increases fluidity)
- Cholesterol is **key to membrane structural stability**

**(iv) Phospholipids — Building blocks of membranes**
- Structure: 1 glycerol + 2 fatty acids + 1 phosphate group + polar head group
- **Amphipathic**: Has both a hydrophilic head (phosphate group) and hydrophobic tails (fatty acid chains)
- **Self-assembly**: Automatically form a lipid bilayer in water — hydrophobic tails face inward, hydrophilic heads face outward
- This is the basis of all cell membranes

## TOPIC 1.6 — Nucleic Acids

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 3 (Information Storage and Transmission)

### 📌 LO 1.6.A
Describe the structure and function of DNA and RNA.

#### 📍 EK 1.6.A.1 — Nucleotides are the monomers of nucleic acids

**Full Explanation:**
- Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) are **nucleotide polymers**
- Biological information is coded in the **sequence** of nucleotides

**Each nucleotide consists of three parts:**
```
        Nitrogenous base (A/T/G/C/U)
              ↑
  Phosphate——Pentose sugar
  (PO₄³⁻)   (ribose/deoxyribose)
```

**(1) Pentose sugar:**
- **Ribose**: C₅H₁₀O₅, all hydroxyl groups in place (in RNA)
- **Deoxyribose**: C₅H₁₀O₄, differs from ribose in that the hydroxyl group on the 2' carbon is replaced by hydrogen ("deoxy")

**(2) Phosphate group:**
- Negatively charged (PO₄³⁻)
- Makes nucleic acid molecules **negatively charged**
- Connects adjacent nucleotides via phosphodiester bonds

**(3) Nitrogenous bases:**
| Base Type | Base Name | In DNA | In RNA |
|-----------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Purines (double-ring)** | Adenine (A) | ✓ | ✓ |
| | Guanine (G) | ✓ | ✓ |
| **Pyrimidines (single-ring)** | Cytosine (C) | ✓ | ✓ |
| | Thymine (T) | ✓ | ✗ |
| | Uracil (U) | ✗ | ✓ |

---

#### 📍 EK 1.6.A.2 — Linear sequence and directionality of nucleic acids

**Full Explanation:**
- Nucleic acids are **linear sequences** — nucleotides arranged one after another
- **Have directionality (polarity)**:
  - **5' end**: Exposed phosphate group (attached to the 5' carbon of the pentose sugar)
  - **3' end**: Exposed hydroxyl group (-OH, attached to the 3' carbon of the pentose sugar)
- **Synthesis direction**: New nucleotides are always added to the **3' end of the growing chain**
  - This is critical in DNA replication and RNA transcription
- **Covalent linkage**: Adjacent nucleotides are connected by a **phosphodiester bond**
  - From the 5' phosphate of one nucleotide to the 3' hydroxyl of the next

---

#### 📍 EK 1.6.A.3 — DNA double helix and base pairing

**Full Explanation:**
- **DNA double helix structure** (Watson & Crick, 1953):
  - Two polynucleotide strands arranged in **antiparallel** fashion
  - One strand's 5'→3' direction is opposite to the other strand's 3'→5' direction
  - The two strands coil around a central axis forming a **double helix**

- **Complementary base pairing (via hydrogen bonds):**
  ```
  In DNA:
  Adenine (A) ≡ Thymine (T) — 2 hydrogen bonds
  Guanine (G) ≡ Cytosine (C) — 3 hydrogen bonds (more stable)
  
  In RNA (during transcription):
  Adenine (A) ≡ Uracil (U) — replaces T
  ```

- **Importance of hydrogen bonds**:
  - Hydrogen bonds between bases are weak, allowing the double strand to **unzip** during replication/transcription
  - G-C pairs (3 hydrogen bonds) are more stable than A-T pairs (2 hydrogen bonds)
  - DNA with high GC content has higher **thermal stability**

---

#### 📍 EK 1.6.A.4 — Structural differences between DNA and RNA

**Full Explanation (detailed comparison):**
| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Pentose sugar** | **Deoxyribose** (-H at 2') | **Ribose** (-OH at 2') |
| **Bases** | A, T, G, C | A, U, G, C |
| **Number of strands** | **Typically double-stranded** | **Typically single-stranded** (can fold on itself) |
| **Stability** | Very stable (long-term storage) | Unstable (rapidly degraded) |
| **Main function** | Long-term genetic information storage | Information transfer, translation, regulation, catalysis |
| **Cellular location** | Mainly in nucleus (small amounts in mitochondria/chloroplasts) | Nucleus, cytoplasm, ribosomes |
| **Length** | Very long (millions of bp) | Short (tens to thousands of nt) |
| **Helix type** | B-form double helix | A-form helix (local) or complex folding |

**RNA's additional complexity:**
- Although typically single-stranded, can form **stem-loop structures** in internally complementary regions
- tRNA folds into a **cloverleaf structure**
- Some RNAs have **catalytic activity** (ribozymes)

## TOPIC 1.7 — Proteins

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 3 (Information Storage and Transmission)

### 📌 LO 1.7.A
Describe the structure and function of proteins.

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.1 — Peptide bonds connect amino acids

**Full Explanation:**
- Proteins are **linear chains of amino acids**
- Two amino acids are joined by a covalent **peptide bond** via **dehydration synthesis**
- **Peptide bond formation location**:
  - The **carboxyl group (-COOH)** of one amino acid reacts with the **amino group (-NH₂)** of the next amino acid
  - One water molecule is removed, forming a -CO-NH- bond
  ```
  Amino acid₁-COOH + H₂N-Amino acid₂ → Amino acid₁-CO-NH-Amino acid₂ + H₂O
                                            ↑
                                         Peptide bond
  ```
- **Polypeptide chain**: A chain of multiple amino acids linked by peptide bonds
- Proteins may consist of one polypeptide chain or multiple polypeptide chains (subunits)

---

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.2 — Universal structure of amino acids

**Full Explanation:**
```
          H
          |
  H₂N — C — COOH
          |
          R (variable side chain)
```

- **Central carbon atom (α-carbon)** connected to 4 groups:
  1. **Amino group (-NH₂)** — basic
  2. **Carboxyl group (-COOH)** — acidic
  3. **Hydrogen atom (-H)**
  4. **R group (side chain)** — determines the chemical properties of the amino acid

**Three Chemical Classifications of R Groups (must master):**

| Type | Property | Examples | Behavior in Proteins |
|------|----------|---------|---------------------|
| **Hydrophobic/Nonpolar** | Insoluble in water | Alanine, Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine, Phenylalanine, Tryptophan, Methionine | Tend to be buried inside the protein, away from water |
| **Hydrophilic/Polar** | Soluble in water | Serine, Threonine, Cysteine, Asparagine, Glutamine, Tyrosine | Tend to be on the protein surface, in contact with water |
| **Ionic (Charged)** | Positively or negatively charged | Lysine(+), Arginine(+), Histidine(+), Aspartic acid(-), Glutamic acid(-) | Form ionic bonds, participate in active site catalysis |

**Key concept**: Interactions of R groups (hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, hydrogen bonds, disulfide bridges) **determine the structure and function of that region of the protein**

---

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.3 — Primary Structure

**Full Explanation:**
- **Definition**: The **linear sequence** of amino acids in a polypeptide chain
- Determined by the **codon sequence in the gene**
- Primary structure determines all higher levels of folding
- **Significance**:
  - Even a single amino acid change can lead to enormous changes in the entire protein's structure and function
  - **Classic example**: Sickle cell anemia — glutamic acid (polar, negatively charged) at position 6 of hemoglobin is replaced by valine (nonpolar) → hemoglobin aggregates forming fibers → red blood cells become sickle-shaped → reduced oxygen-carrying capacity

---

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.4 — Secondary Structure

**Full Explanation:**
- Formed by **local folding** of atoms in the polypeptide backbone
- **Does not involve R groups** — driven entirely by interactions between -CO- and -NH-
- Formed via **hydrogen bonds** between:
  - The **carbonyl oxygen (C=O)** of one amino acid
  - The **amide hydrogen (N-H)** of another amino acid several residues away on the same chain

**Two main types:**

**(1) Alpha-Helix:**
- Right-handed spiral of the polypeptide chain
- 3.6 amino acid residues per full turn
- Maintained by hydrogen bonds between n → n+4
- Commonly found in transmembrane proteins (hydrophobic helices spanning the cell membrane)
- Abundant in keratin (hair, nails)

**(2) Beta-Pleated Sheet:**
- Polypeptide chain arranged in a zigzag pattern
- Adjacent segments connected by hydrogen bonds, forming a "folded" sheet
- Can be **parallel** (segments running in the same direction) or **antiparallel** (segments running in opposite directions)
- Common in structural proteins (e.g., fibroin in silk)

---

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.5 — Tertiary Structure

**Full Explanation:**
- **Definition**: The **three-dimensional spatial configuration** of the entire polypeptide chain
- Determined by various interactions between R groups:

**Four main interactions:**

| Interaction Type | Groups Involved | Strength | Description |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------|-------------|
| **Hydrophobic interactions** | Nonpolar R groups | Weak (but numerous) | Nonpolar R groups cluster in the protein's interior, away from water — this is the main driving force of protein folding |
| **Hydrogen bonds** | Between polar R groups or between R groups and backbone | Weak to moderate | Provide directionality and specificity |
| **Ionic bonds (salt bridges)** | Between positively and negatively charged R groups | Moderate | Electrostatic attraction (e.g., between Lys⁺ and Asp⁻) |
| **Disulfide bridges** | -SH groups of two cysteines | **Strongest (covalent)** | Form -S-S- bonds, stabilize protein folding; only found in extracellular proteins (intracellular environment is reducing, which breaks disulfide bonds) |

**Denaturation:**
- When temperature, pH, or chemical environment changes, the tertiary structure of a protein is disrupted
- Hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, and hydrophobic interactions are broken
- But **peptide bonds are not broken** (primary structure remains intact)
- Denaturation usually leads to **loss of function**
- In some cases, denaturation is reversible (renaturation)

---

#### 📍 EK 1.7.A.6 — Quaternary Structure

**Full Explanation:**
- **Definition**: Interactions between multiple polypeptide chains (called **subunits**)
- Not all proteins have quaternary structure (only proteins composed of multiple chains)

**Classic examples:**
- **Hemoglobin**:
  - 4 polypeptide chains: 2 α chains + 2 β chains
  - Each chain contains one **heme group** (containing Fe²⁺)
  - The 4 chains work cooperatively to bind/release oxygen
  - **Cooperativity**: One subunit binds O₂ → conformational change → increased affinity for O₂ in other subunits

- **Myoglobin**: Only one chain → has only tertiary structure, no quaternary structure

**Summary — The Four Levels of Protein Structure:**
```
Primary (amino acid sequence) → Secondary (α-helix/β-sheet) → Tertiary (3D folding) → Quaternary (multi-subunit assembly)
    (determined by genes)         (driven by backbone H-bonds)    (R group interactions)   (between subunits)
```

Explain how the structure and function of subcellular components and organelles contribute to the function of the cell.

---

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.1 — Ribosomes

**Full Explanation:**

**Structural features:**
- **Composition**: **Ribosomal RNA (rRNA, ~60%) + Protein (~40%)**
- **Not membrane-bound** — no membrane envelope
- Size:
  - Eukaryotic ribosomes: **80S** (large subunit 60S + small subunit 40S)
  - Prokaryotic ribosomes: **70S** (large subunit 50S + small subunit 30S)
- **Present in all forms of life** — from bacteria to humans
- This reflects the **common ancestry of all known life**

**Function:**
- **Protein synthesis** — linking amino acids into polypeptide chains according to mRNA sequence information
- During translation: mRNA passes through the small subunit, tRNA brings amino acids into the large subunit

**Two distributions in cells:**
| Location | Destination of synthesized proteins |
|----------|-------------------------------------|
| **Free ribosomes** (in cytoplasm) | Proteins used within the cytoplasm (e.g., cytoplasmic enzymes, cytoskeletal proteins) |
| **Bound ribosomes** (attached to rough ER) | Secretory proteins, membrane-embedded proteins, lysosomal proteins |

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.3 — Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

**Full Explanation:**

**Common functions:**
- **Mechanical support**: The ER network helps maintain cell shape
- **Intracellular transport**: Provides channels for material movement within the cell

**Rough ER:**
- Surface attached with **membrane-bound ribosomes** → "rough" appearance
- **Functions**:
  - **Compartmentalization** of proteins — sequestering nascent proteins within the ER lumen
  - **Protein synthesis** — especially secretory and membrane proteins
  - **Folding** of nascent polypeptide chains within the ER lumen
  - **Initial glycosylation** within the ER lumen
- **Highly developed in**: Secretory cells (e.g., pancreatic acinar cells — secrete digestive enzymes) have abundant rough ER

**Smooth ER:**
- No ribosomes on the surface
- **Functions**:
  - **Lipid synthesis** — synthesis of phospholipids and steroid hormones
  - **Detoxification** — in liver cells, modifies and neutralizes toxic substances (e.g., drugs, alcohol)
  - **Calcium ion storage** — in muscle cells, the smooth ER (sarcoplasmic reticulum) stores and releases Ca²⁺ to trigger muscle contraction
- **Distribution**: Abundance varies among cell types (hepatocytes have abundant smooth ER)

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.4 — Golgi Complex

**Full Explanation:**

**Structure:**
- Composed of a stack of **flattened membrane sacs (cisternae)** (typically 4-8)
- Has **polarity**:
  - **Cis face**: Receives transport vesicles from the ER (near nucleus/ER)
  - **Medial face**: Processing occurs
  - **Trans face**: Sorts and packages processed products for departure (near plasma membrane)

**Two main functions:**

**(i) Proper folding and chemical modification of newly synthesized cell products:**
- **Glycosylation** — addition/modification of sugar chains
  - Forms glycoproteins
  - Modifications determine the **function or targeting** of proteins

**(ii) Packaging proteins for transport:**
- Packages modified proteins into **transport vesicles**

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.5 — Mitochondria

**Full Explanation:**

**Structural features (double membrane):**
| Region | Feature | Function |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Outer membrane** | Smooth, contains porins (allows small molecules to pass freely) | Separates mitochondria from cytoplasm |
| **Intermembrane space** | Between outer and inner membranes | Accumulates H⁺ (proton gradient) |

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.6 — Lysosomes

**Full Explanation:**

**Structure:**
- Membrane-bound vesicles
- Interior contains ~40 different **hydrolytic enzymes**
- These enzymes are most active in an **acidic environment (pH ~5)**

**Functions:**

**(1) Digestion of materials:**
- Digestion following **phagocytosis** (e.g., macrophages engulfing bacteria)
- **Autophagy** — recycling damaged organelles (aging mitochondria, etc.)
- Breakdown of macromolecules into monomers (amino acids, monosaccharides, nucleotides) for cellular reuse

**(2) Programmed cell death (Apoptosis):**
- When a cell receives apoptotic signals, lysosomes release hydrolytic enzymes into the cytoplasm
- The cell is digested from within
- Different from **necrosis** — apoptosis is orderly and does not trigger inflammation

#### 📍 EK 2.1.A.7 — Vacuoles

**Full Explanation:**

**In plant cells — Central Vacuole:**
- Occupies **80-90%** of cell volume

**In animal cells:**
- Vacuoles are **relatively small and numerous**
- Function: **Storage of cellular materials** (water, nutrients, waste)

## TOPIC 2.2 — Cell Size

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.2.A
Explain the effect of surface area-to-volume ratio on the exchange of materials between cells/organisms and the environment.

#### 📍 EK 2.2.A.1 — SA:V ratio affects material exchange capacity

**Full Explanation:**

**Core concept**:
As an object (cell) increases in size, **volume increases faster than surface area**

**Mathematical relationship:**
- For a spherical cell: $SA = 4\pi r^2$, $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$
- SA:V = $(4\pi r^2)/(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3) = 3/r$
- **Conclusion**: Radius increases → SA:V ratio **decreases**

**Example calculation:**
| Radius (r) | Surface Area (SA) | Volume (V) | **SA:V** |
|------------|------------------|------------|---------|
| 1 | 12.57 | 4.19 | **3.0** |
| 2 | 50.27 | 33.51 | **1.5** |
| 3 | 113.10 | 113.10 | **1.0** |
| 4 | 201.06 | 268.08 | **0.75** |

**Four key processes affected by SA:V:**
1. **Acquisition of essential nutrients**
2. **Elimination of waste products**
3. **Acquisition or dissipation of thermal energy** (temperature regulation)
4. **Chemical and energy exchange with the environment**

**Adaptive structures in biological evolution (examples of increased surface area):**
| Structure | System | Function |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Root hairs** | Plant roots | Increase surface area for water and mineral absorption |
| **Microvilli** | Small intestine epithelial cells | Increase surface area for nutrient absorption |
| **Cristae** | Mitochondrial inner membrane | Increase surface area for ATP synthesis |
| **Thylakoid stacking** | Chloroplasts | Increase surface area for light capture |
| **Alveoli** | Lungs | Increase surface area for gas exchange |
| **Stomata** | Plant leaves | Control gas exchange |
| **Cilia** | Respiratory tract/fallopian tubes | Move materials |
| **Guard cells** | Plant leaves | Regulate stomatal opening/closing |

---

#### 📍 EK 2.2.A.2 — SA:V limitations on cells and organisms

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Plasma membrane surface area must be sufficient for material exchange**

**(ii) SA:V can limit cell size and shape:**
- **Smaller cells typically have higher SA:V ratios**
- **Higher SA:V** → more efficient material exchange with the environment
- This explains why most cells are **microscopic** (10-100μm diameter)
- If cells become too large, internal materials take too long to diffuse to the surface

**(iii) Increased cell volume → decreased SA:V → increased demand on internal resources:**
- **Critical problem**: When cells become too large, the plasma membrane area is insufficient to meet the enormous metabolic demands of the interior
- Cells solve this through several methods:
  - Maintaining small size
  - Developing membrane folding (e.g., mitochondrial inner membrane)
  - Developing transport systems (e.g., ER network)
  - Forming multicellular structures

**(iv) As organisms increase in size, decreased SA:V affects heat exchange:**
- **Small organisms** (e.g., mice): High SA:V → rapid heat exchange with environment → rapid heat loss → **high metabolic rate** to compensate
- **Large organisms** (e.g., elephants): Low SA:V → slow heat exchange → good heat retention → **lower metabolic rate**
- **Allen's Rule**: Mammals in cold regions have shorter limbs (lower SA:V, reducing heat loss)

**(v) Negative correlation between metabolic rate and body size:**
- Small animals have **higher** metabolic rates per unit body mass than large animals
- Example: **Shrews** need to consume several times their body weight in food daily, while elephants only need ~5% of their body weight
- Key formulaic relationship: Metabolic rate ∝ **mass^(3/4)** (Kleiber's Law)

## TOPIC 2.3 — Plasma Membrane

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.3.A
Describe the role of cell membrane components in maintaining the internal environment of the cell.

#### 📍 EK 2.3.A.1 — Amphipathic nature of phospholipids

**Full Explanation:**

**Phospholipid structure:**
```
        Hydrophilic head (phosphate group + polar group)
              │
        Glycerol backbone
         ╱    ╲
   Hydrophobic tail₁   Hydrophobic tail₂ (fatty acid chains)
```

- **Hydrophilic region (head)**: Polar phosphate group → faces water environment
- **Hydrophobic region (tail)**: Nonpolar fatty acid chains → faces membrane interior

**Arrangement in the membrane:**
- **Bilayer structure**: Two phospholipid layers arranged tail-to-tail
  - Hydrophilic heads face outward (facing extracellular fluid or cytoplasm)
  - Hydrophobic tails face inward (toward each other)
- This forms an **efficient spontaneous barrier**

---

#### 📍 EK 2.3.A.2 — Properties of membrane proteins

**Full Explanation:**

Membrane proteins can be classified according to their mode of interaction with the membrane:

**(1) Integral Proteins:**
- Embedded in the membrane
- Have **both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions**
  - **Hydrophilic regions**: Exposed to the cytoplasm or lining the interior of channels on the external surface
  - **Hydrophobic regions**: Interact with fatty acid chains in the membrane interior

**(2) Peripheral Proteins:**
- Attached to the membrane surface (not embedded)
- Through connection with other membrane proteins or phospholipid heads

**Functions of membrane proteins:**
| Type | Function |
|------|----------|
| Channel proteins | Form hydrophilic pores allowing specific ions to pass through |
| Carrier proteins/transporters | Selectively bind and transport specific molecules |
| Receptor proteins | Bind ligands, triggering cellular responses |
| Enzymes | Catalyze reactions on the membrane surface |
| Linker proteins | Connect to cytoskeleton or extracellular matrix |
| Recognition proteins | Cell recognition (glycoproteins) |

---

### 📌 LO 2.3.B
Describe the fluid mosaic model.

#### 📍 EK 2.3.B.1 — Fluid Mosaic Model

**Full Explanation:**

**Proposed by**: Singer & Nicolson (1972)

**Core components:**
| Component | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Phospholipids** | Bilayer arrangement | **Basic structural framework** |
| **Proteins** | Embedded or peripheral | Transport, signaling, catalysis, connection |
| **Cholesterol** (vertebrates) | Embedded between phospholipids | **Regulates membrane fluidity** and stability |
| **Glycoproteins** | Protein + sugar chain | **Cell recognition**, signal reception |
| **Glycolipids** | Lipid + sugar chain | Cell-cell recognition |

**Meaning of "Fluid":**
- Phospholipids and proteins can **diffuse laterally** within the plane of the membrane
- Lipid rafts — microdomains where specific components aggregate
- Fluidity is affected by:
  - **Temperature**: High temperature increases fluidity, low temperature decreases it
  - **Fatty acid composition**: More unsaturated fatty acids → greater fluidity
  - **Cholesterol**: Decreases fluidity at high temperatures, increases fluidity at low temperatures

**Meaning of "Mosaic":**
- Various proteins are "embedded" in the phospholipid matrix like a **mosaic collage**
- Uneven distribution — different regions have different protein compositions

**Experimental evidence**:
- **Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP)** experiments demonstrated membrane fluidity
- After bleaching fluorescent markers on the membrane, unbleached fluorescent molecules from surrounding areas diffused into the bleached area, proving that fluorescent molecules can move

## TOPIC 2.5 — Membrane Transport

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.5.A
Describe the mechanisms that organisms maintain for solute and water molecule balance.

#### 📍 EK 2.5.A.1
- Selective permeability allows the establishment of **concentration gradients** across the membrane

#### 📍 EK 2.5.A.2 — Passive Transport

**Full Explanation:**

**Definition**: Net movement of substances from **high concentration region → low concentration region** (down the concentration gradient)
**Does not require direct metabolic energy input**

**Two types:**
| Type | Requires protein? | Examples |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **Simple diffusion** | ❌ No | O₂, CO₂ passing through the membrane |
| **Facilitated diffusion** | ✅ Yes, requires channel/carrier proteins | Glucose through GLUT carriers |

**Characteristics of passive transport:**
- Greater concentration difference → faster diffusion rate
- Eventually reaches **dynamic equilibrium** (net flow is zero, but molecules are still moving)
- Higher temperature → higher diffusion rate (increased kinetic energy)

#### 📍 EK 2.5.A.3 — Active Transport

**Full Explanation:**

**Definition**: Requires **direct metabolic energy input (ATP)** to move molecules
Can be used to move molecules from **low concentration → high concentration** (against the concentration gradient)

**Characteristics of active transport:**
- **Requires energy** (typically from ATP hydrolysis)
- **Requires membrane proteins** (carriers)
- **Can transport against concentration gradient**
- **Connected to metabolic state** — active transport stops when cells lack energy (e.g., under hypoxia)

**Classic example — Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (Sodium-Potassium Pump):**
Per 1 ATP consumed:
- Pumps **3 Na⁺** out of the cell
- Pumps **2 K⁺** into the cell
- Result: **low Na⁺, high K⁺** inside the cell; **high Na⁺, low K⁺** outside
- This is critical for **maintaining membrane potential, regulating cell volume, and providing driving force for secondary active transport**

### 📌 LO 2.5.B
Describe the mechanisms organisms use to transport large molecules across the plasma membrane.

#### 📍 EK 2.5.B.1 — Endocytosis and Exocytosis

**Full Explanation:**

**Both processes require energy (ATP):**

**(1) Endocytosis — Cell "eating"**
- Plasma membrane **folds inward**, engulfing external material
- Forms an **endocytic vesicle**
- Three types:

| Type | Contents | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Phagocytosis** | Large particles/pathogens | Cell extends pseudopodia to engulf particles, forming a large vesicle ("cell eating") |
| **Pinocytosis** | Fluid/solutes | Cell membrane invaginates to take in extracellular fluid ("cell drinking") |

**(2) Exocytosis — Cell "expelling"**
- Intracellular vesicle fuses with the plasma membrane
- Vesicle contents **released to the exterior**

**Biological examples:**
| Process | Type | Function |
|---------|------|----------|
| Neurotransmitter release | Exocytosis | Synaptic signal transmission |

## TOPIC 2.7 — Tonicity and Osmoregulation

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.7.A
Explain how concentration gradients affect the movement of molecules across membranes.

#### 📍 EK 2.7.A.1 — Osmosis and Tonicity

**Full Explanation:**

**Osmosis:**
- Net movement of water through a **selectively permeable membrane** from a region of **higher water potential** to a region of **lower water potential**
- Water movement can be described as from **hypotonic → hypertonic** regions

**Three tonicity environments:**

**(1) Isotonic solution:**
- External solute concentration = internal solute concentration
- Water enters and exits at equal rates → **cell size unchanged**
- **Animal cells**: Normal shape
- **Plant cells**: Normal state

**(2) Hypotonic solution:**
- External solute concentration < internal solute concentration
- External water potential is higher → water **net influx** into cell
- **Animal cells**: Water enters → swelling → possible **lysis**
- **Plant cells**: Water enters → swelling → cell wall resists expansion → cell becomes **turgid**

**(3) Hypertonic solution:**
- External solute concentration > internal solute concentration
- External water potential is lower → water **net efflux** from cell
- **Animal cells**: Water exits → **crenation** (shrinking)
- **Plant cells**: Water exits → **plasmolysis** — plasma membrane detaches from cell wall

**Water Potential Formula:**
$$\Psi = \Psi_P + \Psi_S$$

- $\Psi$ = Water potential (measured in bars or megapascals)
- $\Psi_P$ = **Pressure potential** (positive for positive pressure, e.g., turgor pressure in plant cells)
- $\Psi_S$ = **Solute potential** (**always negative or zero**, solutes reduce the free energy of water)
- Water moves from **higher water potential** (larger value, less negative) to **lower water potential** (smaller value, more negative)

**Solute Potential Formula:**
$$\Psi_S = -iCRT$$

| Parameter | Meaning | Typical Values/Units |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| $i$ | **Ionization constant** | Sucrose = 1.0 (does not ionize); NaCl ≈ 2.0 (fully ionizes) |
| $C$ | **Molar concentration** | mol/L |
| $R$ | **Pressure constant** | 0.0831 L·bar/mol·K |
| $T$ | **Absolute temperature** | K = °C + 273 |

**Examples (Illustrative Examples):**
- **Contractile Vacuole**: In freshwater protists, the cell continuously absorbs water (internal hypertonic), and the contractile vacuole periodically expels excess water to maintain osmotic balance
- **Central Vacuole**: Plant cells store solutes and water in the central vacuole, maintaining turgor pressure

## TOPIC 2.8 — Mechanisms of Transport

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.8.A
Describe the processes that enable ions and other molecules to move across membranes.

#### 📍 EK 2.8.A.1 — Active transport and membrane potential

**Full Explanation:**

**Requirements for active transport:**
- Requires **metabolic energy (ATP)** — or other forms of energy (such as light energy or electron transport)
- Used for transport **against concentration gradient**
- Requires **membrane proteins**

**Mechanism of the Na⁺/K⁺ Pump (Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase):**
```
Extracellular: [Na⁺] high, [K⁺] low        ← 3 Na⁺ pumped out
           ↕                       2 K⁺ pumped in →
Intracellular: [Na⁺] low, [K⁺] high
```

1. 3 Na⁺ from inside the cell bind to the pump
2. ATP hydrolysis releases energy → pump changes conformation
3. 3 Na⁺ are released outside the cell
4. 2 K⁺ bind from outside the cell
5. Pump returns to original conformation → K⁺ released inside the cell

**Results:**
- Maintains **concentration gradients of Na⁺ and K⁺ across the membrane**
- **Membrane Potential**: The cell interior is negatively charged relative to the exterior (~ -70mV)
  - Primarily from K⁺ leakage (K⁺ exits down its concentration gradient → positive charge outflow)
  - And the Na⁺/K⁺ pump (3 positive charges pumped out, 2 pumped in → net one positive charge pumped out)
- Membrane potential is the basis for **nerve signal conduction, muscle contraction**, etc.

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 2 (Energetics)

### 📌 LO 2.9.A
Describe the membrane-bound structures of eukaryotic cells.

#### 📍 EK 2.9.A.1
- Membranes and membrane-bound organelles **isolate intracellular metabolic processes and specific enzymatic reactions**
- This allows different reactions to proceed under their own optimal conditions

### 📌 LO 2.9.B
Explain how internal membranes and membrane-bound organelles contribute to compartmentalization of eukaryotic cell functions.

#### 📍 EK 2.9.B.1 — Two major advantages of compartmentalization

**Full Explanation:**

**Advantage 1: Minimizing competitive interactions**
- Incompatible reactions are physically separated
- For example:
  - Lysosomal hydrolytic enzymes (require acidic environment) are isolated from the neutral pH of the cytoplasm
  - Lipid synthesis (smooth ER) is isolated from protein synthesis (rough ER)
  - Protein synthesis is separated from protein degradation (lysosomes)
  - Mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (requires oxygen) and fermentation (does not require oxygen) — though both exist in the same cell, their locations differ

**Advantage 2: Increasing reaction surface area**
- Membrane folding increases the **surface area** available for specific reactions
- For example:
  - Mitochondrial inner membrane folds into cristae → increases the number of ETC and ATP synthase
  - Thylakoid stacking → increases surface area for light capture
  - Rough ER membrane → provides attachment surface for ribosomes

## TOPIC 2.10 — Origins of Cell Compartmentalization

**Related Big Idea:** Big Idea 1 (Evolution)

### 📌 LO 2.10.A
Describe the similarities and differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells in terms of compartmentalization.

#### 📍 EK 2.10.A.1 — Endosymbiosis Theory

**Full Explanation:**

**Proposed by**: Lynn Margulis (1960s)

**Core claim:**
- Mitochondria and chloroplasts originated from **free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells**
- After being engulfed by the host cell, they were not digested but formed a **symbiotic relationship**
- The prokaryotes provided the host with energy (ATP/carbohydrates)
- The host provided the prokaryotes with protection

**Supporting evidence (must master):**
| Evidence | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Double membrane** | Outer membrane from the host cell's vesicle, inner membrane from the prokaryote's plasma membrane |
| **Circular DNA** | Mitochondrial and chloroplast DNA is **circular**, similar to bacterial DNA |
| **70S ribosomes** | Mitochondrial and chloroplast ribosomes are **70S** (like bacteria), not 80S (eukaryotic cytoplasm) |
| **Independent division** | They reproduce by **binary fission**, not synchronized with the host cell cycle |
| **Similar size** | Mitochondria are similar in size to typical bacteria (~1-5μm) |
| **Genome** | DNA sequences of mitochondrial and chloroplast genomes show high similarity to certain bacteria |

#### 📍 EK 2.10.A.2 — Internal organization of prokaryotic cells
- Prokaryotes **generally lack internal membrane-bound organelles**
- But have **internal regions with specialized functions**:
  - **Nucleoid region**: Area where DNA is concentrated
  - Some prokaryotes have **thylakoid membranes** (photosynthetic bacteria)
  - Ribosomes dispersed in the cytoplasm

#### 📍 EK 2.10.A.3 — Internal membranes of eukaryotic cells
- Eukaryotic cells have **extensive internal membranes**, dividing the cell into **specialized regions**
- This allows highly complex cellular functions

## TOPIC 3.1 — Enzymes

### 📌 LO 3.1.A
Explain how enzymes affect the rate of biological reactions.

#### 📍 EK 3.1.A.1 — Enzyme structure and function

**Full Explanation:**

**Nature of enzymes:**
- The vast majority of enzymes are **proteins**
- A small number of RNA molecules also have catalytic activity, called **ribozymes**

**Function of enzymes:**
- **Biological catalysts** — accelerate chemical reactions without being consumed
- Achieved by **lowering Activation Energy (Ea)**

**Concept of activation energy:**
- Chemical reactions need energy to break existing chemical bonds in reactants
- Activation energy is the minimum energy needed for reactants to reach the **transition state**
- Enzymes lower activation energy but do **not change the thermodynamic equilibrium** of the reaction (ΔG unchanged)

**Diagram of enzyme action mechanism:**
```
Energy
  ↑
  │       ╱  Activation energy without enzyme (high)
  │      ╱
  │     ╱   ─── Activation energy with enzyme (low)
  │    ╱        \
  │ ╱            \
  │╱              \   Reaction products
  └────────────────────→ Reaction progress
      Reactants        
```

#### 📍 EK 3.1.A.2 — Enzyme-substrate complex

**Full Explanation:**

**Basic concepts:**
- **Substrate**: The molecule that an enzyme acts upon
- **Active Site**: The specific region on an enzyme where the substrate binds
- **Enzyme-Substrate Complex**: The temporary structure formed when the substrate binds to the enzyme's active site

**Two classical models:**

**(1) Lock-and-Key Model:**
- The active site of an enzyme has a **fixed shape**
- The substrate's shape must be **precisely complementary** to the active site
- Analogy: Key (substrate) opens lock (enzyme)
- Too rigid, cannot fully explain reality

**(2) Induced Fit Model — More accurate:**
- Upon substrate binding, the enzyme's active site shape **undergoes a slight change**
- This enables the enzyme to "wrap around" the substrate more tightly
- Similar to a glove changing shape when receiving a hand
- This stabilizes the transition state, further lowering activation energy

**Important characteristics of enzymes:**
| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Specificity** | Each enzyme typically catalyzes only one type or class of chemical reaction |
| **Not consumed** | Enzymes can be reused |
| **Saturability** | Maximum reaction rate reached when all active sites are occupied (Vmax) |
| **Regulated** | Can be regulated through various means (feedback inhibition, phosphorylation, etc.) |

## TOPIC 3.2 — Environmental Impacts on Enzyme Function

### 📌 LO 3.2.A
Explain how changes to the structure of an enzyme affect its function.

#### 📍 EK 3.2.A.1 — Effects of structural changes in enzyme molecules

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Protein Denaturation:**
- **Definition**: The native conformation of a protein (including enzymes) is disrupted, causing loss of function
- **Causes**:
  | Factor | How it affects enzymes |
  |--------|------------------------|
  | **Excessive temperature** | Breaks hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions that maintain secondary/tertiary structure |
  | **Extreme pH** | Changes the ionization state of R groups, disrupting ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds |
  | **Chemical environment** | Heavy metals, organic solvents, reducing agents disrupt intermolecular forces |
- **Result**: Active site shape is destroyed → substrate can no longer bind → **catalytic function lost**
- When denaturation occurs due to temperature, **primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact**

**(ii) Temperature and pH outside optimal range:**
- Each enzyme has an **optimal temperature** and **optimal pH**
- **Deviation from optimal values**:
  - Slight deviation → activity **decreases**
  - Severe deviation → **denaturation** (irreversible or reversible)
- **Examples**:
  - Most human enzymes have **optimal temperature ≈ 37°C** (human body temperature)
  - Pepsin optimal pH ≈ **2** (stomach acid environment)
  - Trypsin optimal pH ≈ **8** (small intestine alkaline environment)

#### 📍 EK 3.2.A.2 — Denaturation can be reversible
- In some cases, when conditions return to normal, denatured enzymes can **refold and regain activity**
- This is called **renaturation**
- But severe denaturation (e.g., prolonged high temperature) is usually irreversible

---

### 📌 LO 3.2.B
Explain how the cellular environment affects enzyme activity.

#### 📍 EK 3.2.B.1 — Substrate and product concentrations

**Full Explanation:**
- **Substrate concentration** → Reaction rate:
  - Low substrate concentration: reaction rate **increases linearly** with substrate increase
  - High substrate concentration: rate **plateaus** (reaching Vmax — enzyme saturation)
- **Product concentration** → **Feedback inhibition** may occur (product inhibits enzyme activity)

**Michaelis-Menten Kinetics Curve:**
```
Reaction rate
  ↑
Vmax ──────────╮
  │            ╰── Saturation phase (all enzymes engaged)
  │         ╱
  │      ╱    ← First-order reaction phase (rate ∝ [S])
  │   ╱
  │╱
  └──────────────────→ Substrate concentration [S]
```

#### 📍 EK 3.2.B.2 — Temperature effects

**Full Explanation:**
- Temperature increase → molecules move faster → **increased collision frequency**
- → Increased rate of enzyme-substrate complex formation → **increased reaction rate**
- But: temperature too high → molecules move too violently → **weak bonds maintaining structure are broken** → **enzyme denaturation**
- Result: an optimal temperature exists; both below and above it reduce activity

**Temperature-Activity Curve:**
```
Reaction rate
  ↑
  │        ╱╲
  │      ╱  ╲
  │    ╱    ╲
  │  ╱      ╲
  │╱        ╲ ← Denaturation zone
  └──────────────→ Temperature
     Optimal temperature
```

#### 📍 EK 3.2.B.3 — Inhibitors

**Full Explanation:**

**(1) Competitive Inhibitors:**
- **Compete with the substrate for the enzyme's active site**
- Structurally similar to the substrate
- Bind **reversibly** to the active site
- **Effect**: Increases the apparent Km (higher substrate concentration needed to reach half-maximal rate)
- **Can be overcome**: Increasing substrate concentration can "wash out" competitive inhibitors
- **Example**: **Methotrexate** — structurally similar to folic acid, blocks dihydrofolate reductase

**(2) Noncompetitive Inhibitors:**
- Bind to an **allosteric site** — a location on the enzyme other than the active site
- Binding causes a **conformational change** in the enzyme, altering the active site shape
- **Effect**: Decreases Vmax (maximum reaction rate reduced)
- **Cannot be overcome by increasing substrate concentration**
- **Example**: Heavy metals (Pb²⁺, Hg²⁺) binding to -SH groups in enzymes

**Inhibitor comparison summary:**
| Feature | Competitive | Noncompetitive |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Binding location | Active site | Allosteric site |
| Relationship to substrate | Competes | Does not compete |
| Apparent Km | Increases | Unchanged |
| Vmax | Unchanged | Decreases |
| Can be overcome by high substrate? | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |

## TOPIC 3.3 — Cellular Energy

### 📌 LO 3.3.A
Describe the role of energy in living organisms.

#### 📍 EK 3.3.A.1
- **All living systems require energy input**

#### 📍 EK 3.3.A.2 — Thermodynamic laws and life

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Energy input must exceed energy loss to maintain order**
- Life is a **highly ordered system**
- Second law of thermodynamics: Entropy (disorder) tends to increase in isolated systems
- Life **does not violate the second law of thermodynamics** — because it is not an isolated system
- Organisms maintain internal order by continuously taking in energy (food/light) and releasing waste (heat, CO₂)

**(ii) Coupling of exergonic and endergonic reactions:**
- **Exergonic reactions**: Release energy (ΔG < 0)
  - Examples: ATP hydrolysis, cellular respiration
- **Endergonic reactions**: Require energy input (ΔG > 0)
  - Examples: Protein synthesis, active transport, muscle contraction
- **Coupling**: Energy released by exergonic reactions directly drives endergonic reactions
- **Role of ATP**: ATP hydrolysis (exergonic) is coupled with energy-requiring processes (endergonic)

**(iii) Loss of energy or order → Death**
- If metabolism stops, the cell cannot maintain its ordered structure → cell death

> ⛔ **Gibbs free energy formula is not within the AP exam scope**

#### 📍 EK 3.3.A.3 — Sequential nature of metabolic pathways

**Full Explanation:**
- Metabolic pathways are a series of **sequential enzyme-catalyzed reactions**
- The **product of one reaction in a pathway is the reactant for the next step**
- **Advantages**:
  - **Stepwise energy release** (rather than all at once — which would be explosive)
  - **Precise regulation** — each step can be individually regulated
  - **Avoids accumulation of harmful intermediates**

**Regulation of metabolic pathways:**
- **Feedback Inhibition**: The end product inhibits an early enzyme in the pathway
  - This is the most common regulatory mechanism in cells
  - Example: Isoleucine inhibits threonine deaminase (in the isoleucine synthesis pathway)

Explain how shared, conserved fundamental characteristics and processes support common ancestry.

#### 📍 EK 3.3.B.1 — Conservation of core metabolic pathways

**Full Explanation:**

**Core conserved pathways:**

## TOPIC 3.4 — Photosynthesis

### 📌 LO 3.4.A
Describe the process of photosynthesis and the structural features of chloroplasts that enable organisms to capture and store energy.

#### 📍 EK 3.4.A.1 — Overview of photosynthesis

**Full Explanation:**

**Overall reaction:**
$$6CO_2 + 12H_2O + \text{light energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 + 6H_2O$$

**(i) Photosynthetic organisms capture light energy:**
- Photosynthetic organisms: Plants, algae, photosynthetic bacteria (cyanobacteria)
- The captured energy is used to fix CO₂ into carbohydrates
- Products are used for biological processes or **storage**

**(ii) Photosynthesis first evolved in prokaryotes:**
- About 2.5 billion years ago, cyanobacteria first evolved photosynthesis

**(iii) Prokaryotic photosynthesis produced an oxygenated atmosphere:**
- The O₂ released by photosynthesis changed Earth's atmosphere
- Led to the "Great Oxidation Event"
- Eventually made aerobic respiration possible

**(iv) Prokaryotic pathways are the basis for eukaryotic photosynthesis:**
- Eukaryotes acquired photosynthetic capacity through **endosymbiosis** (chloroplasts derived from cyanobacterium-like prokaryotes)

---

#### 📍 EK 3.4.A.2 — Chloroplast structure

**Full Explanation:**

**Chloroplast structure diagram:**
```
  ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │ Outer membrane          │
  │  ┌───────────────────┐  │
  │  │ Inner membrane    │  │
  │  │  ┌─────┐  ┌─────┐ │  │
  │  │  │Thyla│  │Thyla│ │  │ ← Grana
  │  │  │koid │  │koid │ │  │
  │  │  │stack│  │stack│ │  │

  │  │    ← Stroma →      │  │
  │  └───────────────────┘  │

```

**(i) Stroma:**
- Fluid inside the inner membrane
- Located **outside** the thylakoids
- Contains enzymes needed for the Calvin cycle (carbon fixation)
- Contains chloroplast DNA and ribosomes

**(ii) Thylakoid Membranes:**
- Contain **chlorophyll** and other photosynthetic pigments
- Pigments are organized into two **Photosystems (Photosystem I and II)**
- Contain **electron transport proteins**
- Thylakoids stack to form **grana**

**(iii) Light reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the grana**

---

#### 📍 EK 3.4.A.3 — Light reactions

**Full Explanation:**
- A series of coordinated reaction pathways
- **Inputs**: Light, H₂O, NADP⁺, ADP+Pi
- **Outputs**: ATP, NADPH, O₂ (byproduct)
- ATP and NADPH provide energy for the Calvin cycle

### 📌 LO 3.4.B
Explain how cells capture energy from light and transfer it to biological molecules.

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.1 — Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

**Full Explanation:**
- ETC reactions exist in **chloroplasts, mitochondria, and the plasma membrane of prokaryotic cells**
- In photosynthesis, electrons are passed through the thylakoid membrane
- The final electron acceptor is **NADP⁺**, reduced to **NADPH** (in Photosystem I)

> ⛔ The **full names of specific electron carriers** in the ETC are not within the AP exam scope

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.2 — Role of chlorophyll

**Full Explanation:**
- Chlorophyll (mainly chlorophyll a) **absorbs light energy**
- The absorbed energy excites electrons to **higher energy levels**
- These high-energy electrons are ejected from Photosystem II (PSII)
- **Water splitting (Photolysis)**:
  $$2H_2O \rightarrow 4H^+ + 4e^- + O_2$$
  - Water provides electrons to replace those lost from PSII
  - O₂ is released as a byproduct into the atmosphere

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.3 — Photosystem I and II

**Full Explanation:**

**Structure of photosystems:**
- Each photosystem consists of an **antenna complex** (pigment molecules that capture light energy) and a **reaction center** (specialized chlorophyll a molecule that converts light energy into chemical energy)

**Z-scheme (Linear electron flow):**
```
        Light
         ↓
  PSII → ETC → PSI → NADP⁺ → NADPH
         ↑
  Water splitting provides initial electrons
```

- PSII → Water splitting → electrons passed to ETC
- ETC → Proton pump → proton gradient
- PSI → Receives electrons from ETC, light energy re-excites electrons
- Final electrons reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.4 — Establishment of proton gradient

**Full Explanation:**
- As electrons pass through a series of redox reactions in the ETC
- Energy is used to pump H⁺ from the stroma into the **thylakoid lumen**
- Result:
  - Thylakoid lumen: **high [H⁺]** (low pH)
  - Stroma: **low [H⁺]** (high pH)
- An **electrochemical proton gradient** is established across the thylakoid membrane

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.5 — Chemiosmosis and Photophosphorylation

**Full Explanation:**
- H⁺ flows back from the thylakoid lumen to the stroma through **ATP synthase**
- The flow of H⁺ back **drives ATP synthesis**:
  $$ADP + Pi \rightarrow ATP$$
- This process of ATP production via chemiosmosis is called **photophosphorylation**

**Mechanism of ATP synthase:**
- H⁺ flows through the head of ATP synthase
- Drives rotation of the head
- Rotation causes conformational changes that catalyze ATP formation

#### 📍 EK 3.4.B.6 — Calvin Cycle

**Full Explanation:**
- Occurs in the **stroma**
- Requires **ATP and NADPH** produced in the light reactions
- **Inputs**: CO₂, ATP, NADPH
- **Outputs**: Carbohydrates (triose phosphate/glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate), ADP, NADP⁺
- Ultimately produces carbohydrates like glucose

**No need to memorize specific steps and enzyme names of the Calvin cycle** (except ATP synthase)

## TOPIC 3.5 — Cellular Respiration

### 📌 LO 3.5.A
Describe the process of mitochondria and structural features that enable organisms to utilize energy stored in biological macromolecules.

#### 📍 EK 3.5.A.1
- Cellular respiration uses energy from macromolecules to **synthesize ATP**
- Respiration and fermentation are characteristics of **all life forms**

#### 📍 EK 3.5.A.2
- Eukaryotic aerobic respiration: a series of **coordinated enzyme-catalyzed reactions**
- **Captures energy** from macromolecules

#### 📍 EK 3.5.A.3 — ETC and Oxidative Phosphorylation

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Electron Transport Chain (ETC):**
- NADH and FADH₂ deliver electrons to the ETC
- Electrons move through a series of electron carriers
- **Final electron acceptor = Oxygen (O₂)**
  $$O_2 + 4e^- + 4H^+ \rightarrow 2H_2O$$
- Aerobic prokaryotes use O₂; anaerobic prokaryotes use other molecules (such as SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻)

**(ii) Proton gradient:**
- Electron transport through the ETC is accompanied by proton pumping across the **mitochondrial inner membrane**
- Intermembrane space: **high [H⁺]** (low pH)
- Matrix: **low [H⁺]** (high pH)
- The inner membrane folds into cristae → increases surface area → allows more ATP synthesis

**(iii) Chemiosmosis and Oxidative Phosphorylation:**
- H⁺ flows back from the intermembrane space to the matrix through **ATP synthase**
- This H⁺ backflow drives ATP synthesis
- This is called **Oxidative Phosphorylation**

**(iv) Uncoupling for heat production:**
- In some cases, electron transport is **uncoupled** from oxidative phosphorylation
- H⁺ flows back through special channels (UCP1/uncoupling proteins) in the inner membrane
- ATP is no longer produced; instead, **heat** is released
- Used for **thermoregulation** (e.g., hibernating animals, brown adipose tissue in newborns)

> ⛔ Specific ETC carrier names, specific steps of the Krebs cycle, etc., are not within the AP exam scope

### 📌 LO 3.5.B
Explain how cells obtain energy from biological macromolecules.

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.1 — Glycolysis

**Full Explanation:**

**Location**: Cytosol
**Oxygen requirement**: **Not required** (anaerobic process)
**Input**: 1 molecule of glucose (6-carbon)
**Output**:
- 2 molecules of pyruvate (3-carbon)
- **Net 2 ATP** (consumes 2 ATP, produces 4 ATP)
- 2 NADH
- 2 H₂O

**Overview**:
$$\text{Glucose (C}_6\text{)} \rightarrow 2\text{ Pyruvate (C}_3\text{)} + 2\text{ATP} + 2\text{NADH}$$

**Significance of glycolysis**:
- The **most ancient** energy acquisition pathway shared by all organisms
- Does not depend on oxygen, can proceed under anaerobic conditions
- The product of glycolysis, pyruvate, is the starting point for the next step of aerobic respiration

> ⛔ No need to memorize the specific steps and enzyme names of glycolysis

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.2 — Pyruvate Oxidation

**Full Explanation:**
- Pyruvate is transported into the mitochondria
- Oxidized in the mitochondrial matrix
- Key events:
  - Each pyruvate loses one CO₂ (decarboxylation)
  - Reduces NAD⁺ to NADH
  - The resulting acetyl-CoA enters the Krebs cycle

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.3 — Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

**Location**: **Mitochondrial matrix**

**Output per molecule of acetyl-CoA:**
- 2 CO₂ (decarboxylation)
- 1 ATP (or GTP)
- 3 NADH
- 1 FADH₂

> ⛔ No need to memorize specific steps and enzyme names of the Krebs cycle

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.4
- Electrons extracted in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle are transferred via **NADH and FADH₂** to the **ETC in the mitochondrial inner membrane**

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.5
- Electron transport in the ETC generates a proton gradient
- **Matrix pH is higher than intermembrane space pH** (matrix is more basic)

#### 📍 EK 3.5.B.6 — Fermentation

**Full Explanation:**

**When oxygen is absent:**
- The electron transport chain stops (no final electron acceptor)
- NADH cannot be regenerated to NAD⁺
- Cells need **fermentation** to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue

**Two main types:**

**(1) Alcoholic Fermentation:**
```
Pyruvate → Acetaldehyde → Ethanol + CO₂
                           ↑ NADH → NAD⁺
```
- Microorganisms: Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)
- Products: Ethanol + CO₂
- **Applications**: Brewing, baking

**(2) Lactic Acid Fermentation:**
```
Pyruvate → Lactic acid
           ↑ NADH → NAD⁺
```
- Organisms: Some bacteria, animal muscle cells
- Products: Lactic acid
- **In humans**: Muscles during intense exercise (oxygen supply insufficient)

**Comparison of Fermentation vs. Aerobic Respiration:**
| Feature | Aerobic Respiration | Fermentation |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| Oxygen | Required | Not required |
| ATP/glucose | ~36-38 | **2** |
| Final electron acceptor | O₂ | Organic molecules (acetaldehyde/pyruvate) |
| End products | CO₂ + H₂O | Ethanol+CO₂ or Lactic acid |
| Energy efficiency | **High** | **Low** |

### 📌 LO 4.2.B

#### 📍 EK 4.2.B.1 — Ligands and Receptors

**Full Explanation:**

**The three basic steps of signal transduction:**

```

    ↓ ② Transduction
```

**(ii) G Protein-Coupled Receptors (GPCRs):**
- One of the largest receptor families
- Structure: 7 transmembrane α-helices
- Mechanism:
  1. Ligand binds to GPCR
  2. GPCR changes conformation, activates G protein (GDP→GTP exchange)
  3. G protein activates downstream effectors (e.g., adenylyl cyclase)

**(iii) Receptor Location:**
- **Cell surface receptors**: For hydrophilic signal molecules (protein hormones, neurotransmitters) — cannot cross the plasma membrane
- **Intracellular receptors**: For hydrophobic signal molecules (steroid hormones, thyroid hormones) — can cross the plasma membrane

#### 📍 EK 4.2.B.2 — Signaling cascades and second messengers

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Receptor conformation change upon ligand binding:**
- The receptor's **intracellular domain** changes shape
- Exposes sites for binding other proteins or activates enzyme activity

**(ii) Second Messengers:**
- Small intracellular signaling molecules
- Transmit signals from receptors to downstream effectors
- **Amplify signals**
- Common second messengers:
  - **Cyclic AMP (cAMP)**: Synthesized from ATP by adenylyl cyclase
  - **Ca²⁺**: Released from the ER or influx from outside the cell
  - **IP₃, DAG**: From cleavage of phosphatidylinositol

**(iii) Long-distance action of hormones:**
- Hormones travel through the bloodstream throughout the body
- Only cells expressing the appropriate receptor respond

**(iv) Ligand-Gated Channels:**
- Ligand binds to the channel protein
- Causes the channel to open or close
- **Example**: Acetylcholine-gated Na⁺ channels at the neuromuscular junction

## TOPIC 4.3 — Signal Transduction Pathways（信号转导途径）

**关联 Big Idea：** Big Idea 3

### 📌 LO 4.3.A
Describe the different types of cellular responses elicited by signal transduction pathways.

#### 📍 EK 4.3.A.1

**Full Explanation:**

**Possible cellular responses include:**
| Response Type | Description | Example |
|---------------|-------------|---------|
| **Changes in gene expression** | Transcription factors activated/inhibited, altering mRNA and protein synthesis | Hormones regulate metabolic enzymes |
| **Changes in cell function** | Enzyme activity changes, cytoskeletal reorganization | Epinephrine promotes glycogen breakdown |
| **Phenotypic changes** | Cell differentiation, morphological changes | Morphogen gradients |
| **Apoptosis** | Programmed cell death | Eliminating "excess" cells during development |

**Important example — Epinephrine signaling cascade:**
1. Epinephrine binds to β-adrenergic receptor (GPCR)
2. G protein activates adenylyl cyclase
3. ATP → cAMP (second messenger, signal amplification)
4. cAMP activates Protein Kinase A (PKA)
5. PKA phosphorylates phosphorylase kinase → activates glycogen phosphorylase
6. Glycogen broken down into glucose-1-phosphate → blood glucose rises

**Yeast Mating Pheromones:**
- Yeast secrete mating pheromones
- Pheromones bind GPCR → MAP kinase cascade → triggers mating gene expression
- Cells stop dividing, prepare to fuse

**Ethylene and Fruit Ripening:**
- Ethylene gas is a plant hormone
- Binds to receptors → signal transduction → activates genes like pectinase
- Fruit softens, sweetens, changes color

### 📌 LO 4.3.B
Explain how changes in signal molecule structure affect the activity of signaling pathways.

#### 📍 EK 4.3.B.1 — Effects of mutations

**Full Explanation:**
- **Mutations in any domain of the receptor protein** affect ligand binding or transduction ability
- **Mutations in any component of the signaling pathway** affect signal transmission to downstream components
- **Results**:
  - Signal overactivation (constantly "on" — may lead to cancer)
  - Signal failure (constantly "off" — may lead to developmental defects)

#### 📍 EK 4.3.B.2 — Chemical interference

**Full Explanation:**
- Chemicals that can interact with any component of a signaling pathway can **activate or inhibit** the pathway
- **Examples**:
  - Some drugs mimic natural ligands (agonists)
  - Some drugs block receptors (antagonists)
  - Caffeine inhibits phosphodiesterase → prolongs cAMP activity

**Examples:**
| Example | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Cytokines** | Regulate gene expression, allow cell replication and division |
| **Yeast mating pheromones** | Trigger mating gene expression |
| **Ethylene** | Level changes alter enzyme production, causing fruit ripening |
| **HOX genes** | Regulate body patterning in animal embryonic development |

## TOPIC 4.4 — Feedback（反馈调节）

**关联 Big Idea：** Big Idea 2（Energetics）

### 📌 LO 4.4.A
Explain how positive and negative feedback help maintain homeostasis.

#### 📍 EK 4.4.A.1 — Feedback mechanisms maintain internal environment

**Full Explanation:**

**(i) Negative Feedback:**

**Core feature**: Reduces or reverses the initial stimulus → maintains stability

**Mechanism:**
```
Stimulus → Deviation from set point → Response → Reduces stimulus → Return to set point
```

**Classic example — Blood glucose regulation:**
```
Blood glucose rises → Pancreas secretes insulin → Cells take up glucose → Liver stores glycogen → Blood glucose decreases
Blood glucose decreases → Pancreas secretes glucagon → Liver breaks down glycogen releasing glucose → Blood glucose increases
```

**Other negative feedback examples:**
| System | Stimulus | Response | Effect |
|--------|----------|----------|--------|
| Body temperature | Temperature rises | Sweating, vasodilation | Heat loss, temperature decreases |
| Body temperature | Temperature drops | Shivering, vasoconstriction | Heat production, temperature rises |
| Blood calcium | Blood calcium decreases | Parathyroid hormone increases | Calcium release, blood calcium rises |
| Blood pressure | Blood pressure rises | Heart rate slows, vasodilation | Blood pressure decreases |

**(ii) Positive Feedback:**

**Core feature**: Amplifies the initial stimulus → drives a process to completion

**Mechanism:**
```
Stimulus → Response → Further amplifies stimulus → Stronger response → ... → Reaches endpoint
```

**Classic example — Childbirth:**
```
Fetus presses on cervix
    ↓
Release of oxytocin
    ↓
Uterine contractions strengthen
    ↓
Fetus further presses on cervix
    ↓
More oxytocin released
    ↓
Stronger contractions... → until baby is born
```

**Other positive feedback examples:**
| Example | Process |
|---------|---------|
| **Lactation in mammals** | Baby suckles → prolactin released → milk production → more suckling… |
| **Fruit ripening** | Ethylene produced → ethylene induces more ethylene synthesis → rapid ripening |
| **Neural action potential** | Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open → depolarization → more channels open |
| **Blood clotting** | Clotting cascade — each step amplifies the initial signal |

**Negative Feedback vs. Positive Feedback Comparison:**
| Feature | Negative Feedback | Positive Feedback |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| Effect | Resists change, restores set point | Amplifies change, moves away from set point |
| Stability | **Maintains homeostasis** | **Disrupts homeostasis** (drives process to endpoint) |
| Frequency | **Most common** regulatory method | Less common |
| Examples | Body temperature, blood glucose, pH | Childbirth, blood clotting, action potentials |
| Result | System returns to **balance** | System reaches **endpoint** then terminates |

## TOPIC 4.5 — Cell Cycle（细胞周期）

**关联 Big Idea：** Big Idea 3（Information）

### 📌 LO 4.5.A
Describe the events that occur in the cell cycle.

#### 📍 EK 4.5.A.1 — Cell cycle phases

**Full Explanation:**

**Cell cycle overview:**
```
             ┌───────── G₁ Phase (Gap 1)──────────┐
             │  Cell growth, protein synthesis,     │
             │  organelle replication, metabolically active │
             │                                      │
             │  ↓ (through G₁ checkpoint)           │
             │                                      │
   G₀ Phase ←──── S Phase (Synthesis)               │
  (non-dividing)   │  DNA replication (chromatin → sister chromatids)│
             │                                      │
             │  ↓                                    │
             │  G₂ Phase (Gap 2)                    │
             │  Protein synthesis, ATP production,   │
             │  Centrosome replication               │
             │                                      │
             │  ↓ (through G₂ checkpoint)            │
             │                                      │
             │  M Phase (Mitosis)                   │
             │  Prophase→Metaphase→Anaphase→Telophase│
             │                                      │
             │  ↓                                    │
             └── Cytokinesis ───────────────────────┘
```

**(i) Sequential phases of the cell cycle:**
- **Interphase**: G₁ → S → G₂
- **M phase (Mitosis)**
- **Cytokinesis**

**(ii) G₁ Phase (Gap 1) — ~10 hours:**
- Cell is **metabolically active**
- Replicates **organelles and cytoplasmic components**
- Synthesizes RNA and proteins
- Cell grows to appropriate size

**(iii) S Phase (Synthesis) — ~9 hours:**
- **DNA replication**
- DNA exists as **chromatin**
- After replication, each chromosome consists of two **sister chromatids**
- Sister chromatids are connected at the **centromere**
- Histones are also synthesized

**(iv) G₂ Phase (Gap 2) — ~4 hours:**
- **Protein synthesis** (especially proteins needed for mitosis like tubulin)
- **Large-scale ATP production**
- **Centrosome replication** (in animal cells)

**(v) G₀ Phase (Gap 0):**
- Cell exits the cell cycle, **no longer dividing**
- Can be **temporary** or **permanent**
- Many differentiated somatic cells enter G₀ (e.g., **neurons, skeletal muscle cells**)
- Certain cells can re-enter the cell cycle upon receiving appropriate signals (e.g., **liver cells** when the liver is damaged)

---

### 📌 LO 4.5.B
Explain how mitosis transmits chromosomes from one generation of cells to the next.

#### 📍 EK 4.5.B.1 — Detailed steps of mitosis

**Full Explanation:**

**Role of mitosis:**
- Ensures the **complete genome** is passed from the parent cell to two **genetically identical** daughter cells
- **Functions**: Growth, tissue repair, **asexual reproduction**

**Phase details:**

**(1) Prophase:**
- **Chromatin condenses** into visible chromosomes (each composed of two sister chromatids)
- **Mitotic spindle** begins to form (composed of microtubules)
- **Centrosomes** (in animal cells) replicate and move toward **opposite poles** of the cell
- **Nuclear envelope** begins to break down
- **Nucleolus** disappears

**(2) Metaphase:**
- Spindle fibers attach to the **centromere** (kinetochore)
- Chromosomes align along the **metaphase plate** (equatorial plane)
- This stage is best for observing chromosome morphology and counting

**(3) Anaphase:**
- **Centromeres of sister chromatids split**
- Spindle fibers **pull individual chromatids (now called chromosomes) toward opposite poles**
- Cell begins to **elongate**

**(4) Telophase:**
- Chromosomes reach the poles and **decondense**
- **Nuclear envelope reforms**
- Mitotic spindle **disassembles**
- **Nucleolus reappears**

**(5) Cytokinesis:**
- **Animal cells**: **Cleavage furrow** — microfilaments (actin) contract, forming a groove in the middle of the cell, eventually "pinching" the cell in two
- **Plant cells**: **Cell plate** — Golgi vesicles fuse at the metaphase plate, forming a new cell wall that separates the two daughter cells

**Summary of mitosis:**
```
1 parent cell (2n, 4N DNA) → 2 daughter cells (each 2n, 2N DNA)
         (Identical, each with one copy of the genome)
```

## TOPIC 4.6 — Regulation of Cell Cycle（细胞周期调控）

**关联 Big Idea：** Big Idea 3（Information）

### 📌 LO 4.6.A
Describe the role of checkpoints in regulating the cell cycle.

#### 📍 EK 4.6.A.1 — Checkpoints

**Full Explanation:**

**Three major checkpoints:**

**(1) G₁ Checkpoint (Restriction Point) — Most important:**
- At the end of G₁ / before S phase begins
- Checks:
  - Is the cell large enough?
  - Are nutrients sufficient?
  - Are growth factors present?
  - Is there DNA damage?
- **If conditions are not met** → cell enters **G₀ phase**
- **If conditions are met** → cell proceeds to S phase

**(2) G₂ Checkpoint:**
- At the end of G₂ / before mitosis begins
- Checks:
  - Is DNA replication **fully** complete?
  - Is there **DNA damage**? If so, repair before proceeding
  - Is the cell large enough?

**(3) M Checkpoint (Spindle Checkpoint):**
- At the metaphase/anaphase transition
- Checks:
  - Are all chromosomes **properly attached to spindle fibers**?
  - Are all chromosomes on the **metaphase plate**?
- If attachment is incorrect → **delays** anaphase until correct

#### 📍 EK 4.6.A.2 — Cyclins and CDKs

**Full Explanation:**

**Core regulatory mechanism:**

**Cyclin-Dependent Kinases (CDKs):**
- Always present in the cell, but **inactive without cyclin**
- Drive the cell cycle forward by phosphorylating target proteins

**Cyclins:**
- Concentrations **fluctuate cyclically**
- Specifically accumulate and degrade at each phase
- Bind to and **activate CDKs**

**Regulatory system:**
```
Cyclin-CDK complex → phosphorylates target proteins → drives cell cycle forward
        ↑
  Cyclin (periodically synthesized and degraded)
```

| Key Cyclin-CDK | Function |
|----------------|----------|
| **Cyclin-CDK (G₁)** | Drives passage through the G₁ checkpoint |
| **Cyclin-CDK (S)** | Initiates DNA replication |
| **Cyclin-CDK (M/MPF)** | Drives entry from G₂ into mitosis |

**MPF (Maturation Promoting Factor):**
- Cyclin B + CDK1
- Initiates mitosis (nuclear envelope breakdown, chromatin condensation, etc.)
- Degraded in anaphase (to end mitosis)

> ⛔ Knowledge of specific Cyclin-CDK pairs or growth factors is not within the AP exam scope

### 📌 LO 4.6.B
Describe the effects of disruptions to the cell cycle on cells or organisms.

#### 📍 EK 4.6.B.1 — Consequences of disruption

**Full Explanation:**

**(1) Cancer:**
- Occurs when cell cycle regulation **loses control**
- Cells divide **uncontrollably**, forming tumors
- **Key genes**:
  - **Proto-oncogenes**: Promote cell division (e.g., Ras)
    - When mutated become **oncogenes** → overactive
  - **Tumor Suppressor Genes**: Inhibit cell division (e.g., p53, Rb)
    - When mutated **lose function** → inhibition no longer exists

**p53 — "Guardian of the Genome":**
- Detects DNA damage at the G₁ checkpoint
- If DNA is damaged:
  - Mild: Activates DNA repair genes
  - Severe: Triggers **apoptosis**
- **p53 mutation**: Damaged cells continue dividing → accumulate more mutations → cancer

**(2) Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death):**
- The **opposite** of cancer — excessive or insufficient apoptosis is also harmful
- **Insufficient apoptosis**: Cancer cells survive (cells that should die continue dividing)
- **Excessive apoptosis**: Neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., Alzheimer's disease)
- **Features of apoptosis**:
  - Cell shrinkage
  - Chromatin condensation
  - DNA fragmentation
  - Formation of apoptotic bodies (cleared by phagocytic cells)
  - **Does not trigger inflammation** (unlike necrosis)
